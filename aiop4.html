ONE — ESKRIMA

After the Solstice — DeSalvo's

Mac put two rattan sticks on the mat.

Jennifer looked at the sticks. She looked at Mac.

"FMA," Mac said. "Filipino Martial Arts. Eskrima, Kali, Arnis — the terms are related. The principles are shared. The core of it is ambidextrous weapon use — both hands, simultaneously, independent." He looked at her. "Pick them up."

She picked them up.

She held them. She felt the weight distribution — rattan, light, roughly the length of her forearm, balanced differently from a bokken, differently again from a sword. She turned them. She felt how they moved.

Her hands moved with them the way her hands moved with drumsticks.

Mac was watching her.

"You've been doing this for years," he said.

She looked at him.

"The independent hand coordination," he said. "Ambidextrous control, different rhythms in each hand simultaneously. You've been training that since you were fifteen." He paused. "I should have seen it sooner."

She looked at the sticks. She looked at her hands.

Drumsticks, she thought. This is drumsticks with consequences.

"Why now," she said.

Mac looked at the mat.

"Because what we found out at the solstice changes the training timeline," he said. "If you're pre-Immortal — if you are what we think you are — then at some point you're going to be in a situation that requires a response. And we don't know when. And we don't know—" he paused. "We don't know if you can die the way a mortal dies. We don't know if you'll come back. We don't know the rules for someone like you because there's never been someone like you." He looked at her. "So we prepare as if the rules are the most dangerous possible version. And we do it now."

She looked at the sticks.

"The katana," she said.

"Still in the curriculum," he said. "But this is better for you. Lighter. Faster. Two weapons means two attack angles simultaneously. Harder to read. Easier to conceal." He looked at her hands. "And you already know how to use them. You just don't know you know yet."

She turned the sticks. She found a grip. She moved through something that was not quite a kata — something that her hands knew from years of a different practice, translated into this context, arriving intact.

Mac watched.

He had the expression of a teacher watching a student arrive at something that had been there all along.

"Yes," he said. "Exactly that."

She went again.

The sticks moved the way she needed them to move.

She filed it in the place where she kept the things that felt like home.

TWO — IS THE DOJO HOLY GROUND?

After the Solstice

She asked it between sets. Not dramatically. The way she asked things that had been sitting in the filing system and had finally risen to the top.

"Is the dojo holy ground," she said.

Mac stopped.

He looked at her.

"DeSalvo's," she said. "Is it holy ground?"

"No," he said.

"Can it be made holy ground," she said.

Mac was quiet for a moment. He looked at the mat. He looked at the dojo.

"It's not—" he started. "The designation isn't something you create. It's inherent to the history of a place. A church is holy ground because of what it's been used for and what it means to the people who use it. A mosque. A burial site. The ground holds the meaning." He paused. "DeSalvo's has been — it's a place of practice. Of genuine practice. Over time—"

"Over time it might be," she said.

"I don't know. I've never encountered a dojo that became holy ground. It's not something I can make happen. I'm not sure anything I can do makes it happen."

She looked at the mat.

"Methos would know," she said.

"Methos would have a theory," Mac said. "Which is different."

She smiled. The small one.

"Why are you asking," he said.

"Because I'm going to be spending a lot of time here," she said. "And I want to understand what I'm in."

Mac looked at her. "What you're in," he said.

"This world," she said. "Your world. My world now. I want to know the edges of it. Where the protections are and where they aren't." She looked at him. "If I can't make the dojo holy ground what do I do instead?"

"You fill it with people who would protect it," he said. "People who know what it is and what happens here and who would not allow the Game to come inside it. You've been doing that since you started sweeping the mat."

She nodded and picked up her sticks."Again?"

"Again."

THREE — MRS. WILLIAMS

DeSalvo's — A Thursday — After the Littles Class

Karen Williams had the look of a woman who had rehearsed.

Mac recognized it. He'd seen it in four hundred years of people who had decided to have a conversation and had prepared for it in the car. She had good posture and a good bag and the specific expression of someone who was going to be reasonable about this.

"Mr. MacLeod," she said. "Do you have a moment."

He had a moment. He brought her into the office. He offered her water. She declined with the small motion of someone who didn't want to be made comfortable. She wanted to be efficient.

"It's about the Littles class," she said.

"Kelly," Mac said.

"Kelly," she confirmed. "I want to start by saying she loves it here. She talks about it constantly. She wants to come every week and on the weeks she can't she's genuinely upset." A pause. She was laying groundwork. "So this isn't about pulling her out."

"I understand," Mac said.

"It's about the instruction," she said. "I've been hearing from Kelly about someone called—" she paused. The pause of a woman saying something she found faintly ridiculous. "Broomie."

Mac waited.

"I assumed it was a nickname for one of the other children," she said. "But when I asked Kelly she said Broomie was her teacher. Her actual teacher." Karen set her bag on her knee more firmly. "I pay Sensei Ryan and yourself for instruction, Mr. MacLeod. I've seen your credentials. Nate's been with you for three years and the difference in him is—" She stopped. She recalibrated. "My point is that I know what proper instruction looks like. And what I'm hearing from Kelly suggests she's being taught by someone who is not on your staff in any formal capacity."

Mac looked at her.

He had the complete answer ready. He also knew it wasn't his to give.

"Mrs. Williams," he said. "The person you're describing is Jennifer Marsh. She is on my staff in a formal capacity. She teaches the Littles class exclusively." He paused. "She's in the building right now. I'd like to ask her to join us, if you're willing. This is a conversation she should be part of."

Karen looked at him with the expression of someone who had expected the conversation to be simpler than it apparently was.

"All right," she said.

* * *

Mac found Jennifer breaking down the Littles mat equipment. She had the foam barriers stacked and was working on the practice weapons, efficient and specific, the way she did everything.

"Office," he said. "Kelly Williams's mother. She has concerns about your instruction. I want you in the room."

Jennifer looked at him.

"I've got your back," he said. "But this one's yours."

She looked at the practice weapon in her hand. She set it down carefully. She looked at the office door.

"Give me thirty seconds," she said.

He gave her thirty seconds.

* * *

She came in and closed the door and introduced herself without waiting. "Jennifer Marsh. I run the Littles program." She sat across from Karen Williams and looked at her with the steadiness of someone who had thought about this kind of conversation before it required thinking about.

"Broomie," Jennifer said. "That's me. Kelly named me that. The other kids picked it up." She looked at Karen. "I was sweeping the mat when Kelly first came in for a trial class. She thought I was the janitor. She told me she liked my broom. By the end of the class she was calling me Broomie." She paused. "I didn't correct it because it made her laugh and because a four-year-old who is laughing is a four-year-old who is going to come back next week."

Karen's expression had shifted slightly. Recalibrating again.

"My concern," Karen said, more carefully now, "is about credentials. Nate was instructed by Sensei Ryan and Mr. MacLeod. I know their backgrounds. I know what they hold." She looked at Jennifer. "What do you hold."

Jennifer didn't look at Mac. She'd known this was coming.

"I'm not a black belt," she said. "I'm not a belt at all in the traditional system. What I am is someone who has trained under Mr. MacLeod's direct supervision for three years in a curriculum he designed, and who was asked by him to run the Littles program because—" She looked at Karen steadily. "Because I'm good at it. Because I understand how small children learn and how to make them feel safe and capable at the same time. And because what Kelly needs at six years old is not what Nate needed at eleven."

"What does Kelly need," Karen said. Not combative. Genuinely asking.

"She needs to know her body is hers," Jennifer said. "She needs to know how to fall without being afraid of the floor. She needs to know the word no is a complete sentence and that she's allowed to use it even when an adult tells her otherwise. She needs to learn how to be loud when loudness is required." Jennifer looked at her. "She's six. If I put her in a formal belt curriculum right now she'll memorize kata and forget why she's there. In three years she'll quit because it stopped feeling like hers." She paused. "Kelly's not going to quit."

Karen Williams was quiet for a moment.

"She told me," Karen said slowly, "that Broomie taught her that her voice was a weapon."

"It's the first thing I teach," Jennifer said.

The quiet that followed was a different kind of quiet than the one before.

Mac had not said a word since Jennifer came in. He did not say one now. He didn't need to.

Karen looked at her bag. She looked at Jennifer.

"Sensei Ryan teaches Nate," she said. "What's his relationship to you?"

"He's my partner," Jennifer said. "He learned to fall on this mat. So did I."

Another pause.

"I'd like to observe a class," Karen said. "If that's permitted."

"Saturday at nine," Jennifer said. "Parents are always welcome to observe but I ask that you stay on the sidelines, for everyone's safety. Come early. Kelly will want to show you the warm-up. She's been working on it."

Karen nodded, stood, shook Mac's hand and then Jennifer's. She had the expression of a woman whose rehearsed conversation had gone somewhere she hadn't prepared for, and who had found the destination reasonable despite herself.

Mac saw her out.

He came back to the office. Jennifer was still sitting there. She had her hands flat on her knees and was looking at the middle distance.

"Nicely done," he said.

"She's going to come Saturday and assess me," Jennifer said.

"Yes," he said.

"That's fair," she said.

"Yes," he said.

She stood. She went back to the practice weapons. She picked up where she'd left off.

Mac stood in the office doorway and watched a woman who had just handled something difficult go back to work without needing anything from the moment but the moment itself.

He filed it in the place where he kept the things that made him certain.

FOUR — SATURDAY AT NINE

DeSalvo's — The Littles Class

Karen Williams arrived at eight fifty-three.

Jennifer was already on the mat. Cross-legged at the center, hands on her knees, eyes closed. She was not performing stillness. She was just still.

Karen stopped in the doorway. She looked at Mac, who had appeared silently beside her in the way he did.

"She starts in meditation," he said quietly. "The kids come in and join her. It takes as long as it takes."

"Where do I—"

"Here is fine," Mac said. "She asked me to remind you — sidelines, for everyone's safety. The children know you're observing. Kelly knows."

Karen nodded. She settled her bag. She watched.

* * *

They came in ones and twos.

Shoes off at the door, which they did without being told. Each one crossed to the mat, found a spot in the circle around Jennifer, and sat. Some of them looked at Jennifer's face first, as if checking the quality of the quiet she was keeping. Then they matched it.

A small boy sat down too hard and caught himself and looked around to see if anyone had noticed. The girl next to him — eight, maybe nine, with the watchful eyes of someone who had been here a long time — gave him the small nod of someone who had done the same thing once and was not going to make anything of it.

Kelly came in at eight fifty-eight. She saw her mother on the sidelines. Her face moved through surprise and then delight and then something more careful — the expression of a child who understood that class was class regardless of who was watching. She crossed to the mat. She found her spot. She sat.

She looked at Jennifer's face. She matched it.

By nine o'clock there were eleven children in the circle. Jennifer sat with them.

Then she opened her eyes.

She stood in one motion, unhurried, and looked around the circle. She looked at each of them. When she'd seen all of them she said:

"Bow in."

They bowed. Not to her. To each other — heads down, hands on knees, the full circle completing itself. Jennifer bowed with them, the same bow, the same depth.

Karen had not expected that.

* * *

The basics moved through the circle the way water moved.

Jennifer demonstrated once. Then she moved. She walked the inside of the circle, unhurried, watching each child work through the sequence. She stopped at a boy whose left hand kept dropping and repositioned it without comment, just a touch and a look, and he corrected and she moved on. She stopped at Kelly, who had the sequence but was holding her breath through it. Jennifer caught her eye and breathed out, demonstratively, and Kelly's shoulders dropped and the technique improved immediately. Jennifer gave her the small nod.

She never raised her voice. She never demonstrated impatience. When someone got something right she said so, precisely, in terms specific enough that the child knew exactly what they'd gotten right and could find it again.

When she corrected she corrected once and moved on. She did not hover.

After twenty minutes the children were warm and focused and nobody had checked the clock.

Karen had not checked her phone.

* * *

"Ichi," Jennifer said.

The children were already moving.

"Ni," she said.

Eleven voices: "San."

"Mizu!" the children said, and the circle dissolved into the organized chaos of eleven small people finding their water bottles and moving toward the fountain in the specific order they'd worked out among themselves, each one counting their turn at the fountain while the others counted with them — "ichi, ni, san" — and stepping aside.

Kelly appeared at the sideline. She was slightly out of breath and entirely pleased with herself.

"Mom," she said. "Did you see the arm thing."

"I saw it," Karen said. "You weren't breathing."

Kelly looked at her with the expression of a child recalibrating her mother.

"Sensei Broomie said that too," Kelly said. "She says the breath is the technique. The rest is just your hands knowing where to go."

She went back for her water bottle.

Karen looked at Mac.

He looked at the mat.

* * *

They came back to the circle without being asked.

Priya — the oldest one, the one who had been watching Karen with the peripheral attention of someone who noticed everything — crossed to the edge of the mat where a canvas bag sat against the wall. She lifted it with the careful hands of someone transporting something of value. She brought it to her spot in the circle and sat with it in her lap.

Jennifer sat down with them.

"Priya," she said.

Priya opened the bag and produced the book.

It was small, spiral-bound, the kind of thing you ordered in a short run and held in both hands. The cover had an illustration of a turtle — careful line work, expressive, clearly done by someone who could draw and who had thought about what a turtle who knew things should look like. The title in Jennifer's handwriting: "The Jade Chronicles."

Karen looked at the book.

"Jade has been practicing courtesy," Jennifer said to the circle. "She thought she understood what it was. She's been saying please and thank you. She holds the door. She waits her turn." She looked at the children. "And then something happened."

"What happened," said the small boy who had sat down too hard.

"Jade's friend Pip was upset," Jennifer said. "Really upset. And Jade said please and thank you. She held the door. She waited her turn." She looked around the circle. "And Pip was still upset."

The children were very quiet.

"Was Jade being discourteous," Jennifer said.

A girl near Kelly shook her head slowly. "She was doing all the right things."

"She was," Jennifer agreed. "So what was missing."

The circle thought about it. Kelly was thinking hard, Jennifer could see it.

"Pip," Kelly said.

Jennifer looked at her.

"She was doing the courtesy things," Kelly said, "but she wasn't doing them — at Pip. She was just doing them."

Jennifer looked at Kelly for a moment.

"Yes," she said. "Exactly that. Courtesy isn't a set of actions. It's attention. It's seeing the person in front of you and letting them know you see them." She looked around the circle. "Jade figured that out. She put down the door-holding and the please and thank you and she just looked at Pip and said: I can see you're having a hard time. Is there something I can do."

"Did it work," Priya said. With the tone of someone who already knew but wanted to hear it said.

"It worked," Jennifer said. "Because Pip felt seen. And feeling seen is the thing courtesy is actually for."

The circle sat with it.

Outside Seacouver went on being Seacouver. Inside, eleven children were thinking about a turtle who had learned something that most adults hadn't.

* * *

The bow-out was the same as the bow-in. The full circle. Everyone to everyone.

After, Kelly ran to her mother without ceremony and attached herself to Karen's side and looked up at her with the expression of someone who had done good work and knew it.

Karen put her hand on Kelly's head.

Jennifer was helping the younger children find their shoes. She was patient about it. She was always patient about it.

Karen crossed the mat line.

"Ms. Marsh," she said.

Jennifer looked up.

"The book," Karen said. "Did you write it."

"My partner drew it," Jennifer said. "I wrote it. We did a small print run."

Karen looked at the copy Priya was carefully returning to the canvas bag.

"Nate is eleven," Karen said. "Is there a version for eleven."

Jennifer looked at her.

"There will be," she said.

Karen nodded. She looked at the mat. She looked at the circle of children pulling on shoes and finding their bags and saying goodbye to each other with the ease of people who had shared something.

"Thank you," Karen said. "For letting me observe."

"Same time every Saturday," Jennifer said. "You're always welcome."

Karen nodded again. She took Kelly's hand.

At the door Kelly turned back.

"Bye Sensei Broomie," she said.

"Same time Saturday, Kel," Jennifer said.

They went down the stairs.

Mac had been standing in the office doorway for the last twenty minutes. He said nothing. Jennifer began breaking down the mat equipment with the efficiency of someone who had done it many times.

After a moment he came and helped.

FIVE — THE DRY CLEANING

DeSalvo's, Joe's Bar — Fall 1993

The sticks were still in her hands when Mac said, "Stay a moment."

She stayed. She set the rattan down on the mat and worked through the cooldown the way she'd been shown. Mac went to the office. She heard the desk drawer.

He came back with a keychain. Celtic shield knot, solid, the kind of thing made to last. Three keys on it.

"Dojo," he said, pointing to the first. "Loft." The second. He held up the third. "The elevator." He looked at her. "Not the T-Bird."

She looked at the keys. She looked at him.

"Never the T-Bird," she said.

"Never," he confirmed.

She took the keychain. She held it in her palm for a moment — the weight of it, the specific gravity of what it meant. She closed her fingers around it.

She went back to breaking down the mat.

* * *

She came in the following Tuesday with something in her jacket pocket. She waited until the session was done. She waited until the others had gone.

She produced the keychain.

It was small. Wooden disc, lacquered, kanji on both sides in black. A flea market thing — the kind of vendor who had a table of them with little paper tags explaining their meanings. She'd spent twenty minutes choosing.

"308," she said. "Front and back."

She handed it to him.

Mac took it. He looked at the keychain. He read it.

He read it again.

Something moved through his expression in the time it took to complete a breath and then it was gone, replaced with the specific warmth of a man receiving something he intended to keep.

"Thank you," he said.

He put it in his pocket.

He went to return Fitz's scissors.

She never asked what it said.

* * *

Richie found out about the keys on a Thursday at Joe's.

He found out the way he found out most things — someone mentioned something incidentally and he did the arithmetic and arrived at a conclusion he had opinions about. In this case the someone was Mac and the something was: "I'll let myself in, Jennifer has the dojo key." And the conclusion was that Mac MacLeod had a key to his girlfriend's apartment and Richie Ryan, who was also her boyfriend, did not.

He raised this at the bar. He raised it with the energy of someone who felt a procedural injustice had occurred and wished it noted for the record.

Jennifer listened to the full presentation. She reached into her bag.

She produced a keychain. Michelangelo. The Ninja Turtle, orange mask, nunchucks, the specific enthusiasm of someone who had considered the options and chosen correctly. One key on the ring.

On the key, in her handwriting: a small heart.

She set it on the bar in front of Richie.

He looked at it.

He picked it up.

He looked at the heart.

He did not say anything further on the subject of procedural injustice.

* * *

She came back the following Tuesday with a different argument.

She had been reading. She presented this upfront, with the specific energy of someone who had done the work and wanted credit for the methodology before the conclusion was rejected.

"The squire," she said, "was responsible for the knight's horse."

Mac looked at her.

"Historically," she said. "Feeding, grooming, tacking. The horse was the knight's primary mode of transport and the squire maintained access to it as a matter of function." She looked at him. "You have a horse."

"I have a T-Bird," Mac said.

"You have a T-Bird," she confirmed. "I should have a key."

Mac looked at the mat. He looked at the dojo. He looked at the specific expression of a woman who had researched this position and was prepared to defend it.

"The argument," he said, "is studied. It is logical. It is not without merit."

"Thank you," she said.

"No," he said.

She looked at him.

"However," he said. "When you need to get oats for the horse you may borrow the keys."

She considered this.

"Borrow," she said.

"No copies," he said.

She filed it in the place where she kept the things that were not finished yet.

"Richie," she said, "is going to lose his mind the first time I drive it."

Mac said nothing.

He went to put the kettle on.

THE BELT QUESTION AND MR. MIYAGI

DeSalvo's — A Tuesday

The belt testing conversation happened on a Tuesday.

She was finishing the sweep when a girl named Maya — nine years old, one of the intermediate students — was sitting on the bench by the door with her shoes on and her bag in her lap and the expression of someone who had decided to wait for something specific.

"Belt testing is in three weeks," Maya said.

"I know," Jennifer said.

"I'm scared," Maya said. Not performing the fear. Just stating it as a fact she had assessed and found true.

"What are you scared of?" Jennifer said.

"Getting it wrong in front of everyone," Maya said. "The parents come and everyone watches and Sensei MacLeod watches and I know the form but when everyone watches I —" She stopped. She found it. "I lose it. My body forgets what it knows."

Jennifer set the broom against the wall. She sat on the bench beside Maya.

"That's a real thing," she said. "It happens to everyone."

"Does it happen to you?" Maya said.

Jennifer thought about the combination she'd gotten wrong in front of Mac seventeen times. About the two seconds her composure had broken in front of half the bar. "Yes," she said. "It happens to me."

"Can you practice with me? Before testing. So I can get used to someone watching."

"Tuesdays after my session with Sensei MacLeod," Jennifer said. "Three sessions, forty-five minutes each."

Maya nodded. She stood. She looked at Jennifer with the specific relief of a child who has asked for something and received it and is now going to go home and practice so as not to waste the opportunity.

"Thank you, Sensei Broomie," she said. She went down the stairs.

Jennifer sat on the bench for a moment. Then she went to find Mac.

* * *

He was in the loft, making tea. She came up the interior stairs and stood in the doorway.

"I agreed to practice with Maya before belt testing," she said. "Tuesdays after our session. Three sessions."

He poured the water. He did not turn around.

"Is that all right?" she said.

He turned around. He looked at her with the complete looking.

"You know enough to teach," he said.

"I'm not teaching," she said. "I'm practicing with her."

"Yes," he said. "The distinction is less meaningful than you think."

She stood in the doorway. "She said her body forgets what it knows when people are watching," she said.

"It's a real problem," he said. "You're the right person to help with it."

She was quiet for a moment.

"Mac," she said. "The parents. When they watch the belt testing." She paused. "I don't have a belt. Should I have a belt? For — for legitimacy. So the parents don't wonder who this person is helping their kids."

Mac looked at her.

He set the tea down.

"I can order one from Century if you want it," he said. "Whatever level you'd like to represent. We can talk about where you actually are and find the honest equivalent."

"But?" she said.

"But," he said, "the belt's primary function is to hold up the pants."

She looked at him.

He had the quality — the one she'd seen perhaps a dozen times, the specific quality of Mac being dry, the version that was so rare it took her a moment to recognize it.

"The Karate Kid," she said.

"You know the movie," he said.

"I know the movie," she said.

What she did not say: she had seen the movie seven times as a child. She had a VHS copy of it at her mother's house in Portland, on the shelf between Pretty in Pink and The Princess Bride, and she had in fact read the novelization before she saw the film because she had been that kind of twelve-year-old, and she had cried at the end of both the book and the movie and was not going to be telling Mac any of this.

"You know the material," Mac said. "The parents will see that. The kids already see it." He picked up his tea. "But if you want the belt, I'll order it."

"No," she said. "No belt."

"Good," he said.

She went back downstairs. She finished the sweep. She thought about Mr. Miyagi telling Daniel that a belt is just to hold up the pants, and she thought about Mac saying it with that same face, and she thought: he's seen the movie.

She thought: of course he's seen the movie.

She thought: I am not going to ask when.

SIX — ZANSHIN

DeSalvo's — The Office — A Tuesday

She brought lunch from the place on the corner — the one with the burritos that were technically too large to be practical and which everyone got anyway because practical was not the point. She had a tray with three of them and a carrier with three coffees and she came through the office door sideways, the way you came through doors when both hands were occupied, and set everything on Mac's desk.

Mac was behind the desk. Richie was in the chair he always occupied when he was in the office, the one at the angle that suggested he'd sat down temporarily and then decided not to leave.

"Lunch," she said.

"Thank you," Mac said.

Richie had already picked up his burrito.

She sat. She distributed the coffees. She unwrapped her own and looked at Mac, who had the expression he had when something was on his mind and had been there for a while.

"Go ahead," she said.

He looked at her.

"You have something to say," she said. "You've had it since I walked in. Go ahead."

Richie stopped chewing briefly. He looked at Mac. He looked at Jennifer. He resumed chewing.

Mac turned his coffee cup.

"I want to put the dojo in your name," he said.

The office was quiet.

"The building," he said. "The business. All of it. I want you to own it formally and legally and on paper." He looked at her steadily. "I've spoken to a lawyer. The process is straightforward."

"Mac," she said.

He waited.

She looked at the burrito. She looked at the desk. She looked at the man across from her who was four hundred years old and had decided, as a practical matter, to give her a building.

"People don't just give each other buildings," she said.

"Immortals with assets and mortal companions have been doing exactly this for as long as there have been legal structures to do it with," Mac said. "It's practical. I'm not always going to be in Seacouver. I'm not always going to be—" He paused. "Available. The dojo needs continuity. You are the continuity."

She turned her coffee cup.

"The classes," she said. "What's been built here. You want it protected in a form that doesn't depend on your presence."

"It already doesn't depend on my presence," he said. "I'm making the paperwork accurate."

She sat with this. Richie was watching her with the specific attention he gave to moments that mattered.

"I'm uneasy," she said.

"I know," Mac said.

"Not because I don't—" She stopped. "It's not ingratitude. It's that it's a building. It's significant and I don't take significant things lightly and I want to make sure I understand what I'm being asked to carry."

"You've been carrying it for two years," Mac said. "I'm asking you to let me make that official."

She looked at the desk. She looked at him.

She looked at it.

She said it again.

"That's the name," Richie said.

"Zed," he said, with four hundred years of Zed behind it.

"Is this still your loft," she said.

THE PRIVATE BELT

Zanshin Dojo — Wednesday, August 19, 1994

The text came in at two-seventeen.

Impromptu session, mandatory, before Joe's. Come as you are.

She looked at it twice. She had just gotten out of her afternoon seminar and had forty minutes before her bar shift. When Ki-san called, she went.

The dojo was empty.

Mac was at the supply closet. That was all — just Mac, the closet, the specific quality of an afternoon dojo with nobody in it. Richie wasn't there. This registered. Richie was usually somewhere in the building at this hour, the specific background presence of him. His absence was noted and filed.

She kicked off her shoes at the threshold. She bowed. She entered.

Mac said nothing. She crossed to the mat, found her position, and lowered herself into seiza — 正座 — the formal sitting, feet tucked, hands on her thighs, spine straight. She closed her eyes.

I am still. I can hear my heartbeat.

She heard Mac move. She tracked him the way she had learned to track movement in a room — without looking, without the eyes, the awareness that had been opening in her since before she had a name for it. He crossed the mat. He stopped in front of her.

She opened her eyes. She stood. She bowed.

He returned the bow.

Then he gestured — a small, specific motion. Her arms. Parallel to the floor.

She moved her arms.

He stepped in.

She understood what was happening at the moment his hands found the belt — the specific black, the weight of it — and she nearly froze. The nearly was all there was. The freeze did not complete itself. She had learned too well the thing he'd taught her about commitment: you go all the way through or you don't go.

She stayed.

Mac worked with the economy of someone for whom this was not a ceremony but a fact being made visible. He tied it correctly — the knot, the tuck, the specific tension of something that was meant to hold. When it was right he gripped both ends and gave a final tug, precise, the period at the end of a sentence.

He stepped back.

He looked at her.

Then he placed his right fist against his left palm.

She understood. She brought her hands together. They bowed — the full bow, head down, the session ending the way all sessions ended.

She straightened.

He had already turned back toward the supply closet.

She stood on the mat in the empty dojo with her arms at her sides and the belt at her waist and the specific quality of an afternoon that had just become one of the ones she was going to keep.

No words had been spoken. It was one of the sessions that said the most.

She bowed at the threshold on the way out, put her shoes on and walked to Joe's.

Jennifer was four minutes early for her shift with a black belt neatly folded in her bag.

BELT TESTING DAY

Zanshin Dojo — A Saturday

Jennifer arrived early as usual to make coffee and train with Mac. Richie arrived with breakfast at 7:30 as he usually did on a Saturday morning. Jennifer started another pot of coffee and wolfed down her meal. It was going to be a big day, one that had been advertised for weeks and she wanted to make sure everything went off without a hitch.

The floor needed to be right, the wood clean and responsive, the corners clear. Jennifer had mopped it the evening before and moved as much of the free weight and other equipment up to the windows. That opened the space beautifully. Everything was in order by 8:15. The building already had the specific buzzing quality of an event that mattered. Parents arrived at eight forty-five. The younger siblings trailed behind with the specific philosophy of younger siblings everywhere: be present, don't get in the way, wait for something interesting.

The students arrived in their uniforms, cleaner than usual, pressed. She recognized this quality because she had laid out her own keikogi the night before her first dojo session with Mac — the specific ritual of preparation that said: I take this seriously.

Maya arrived at eight fifty-two and found Jennifer by the supply corner and said nothing. Jennifer looked at her and nodded once — the acknowledgment that said: you know the form. Your body knows the form. Let it. — and Maya nodded back and went to join the other students lining up.

The testing itself was precise and sequential. Mac ran it with the quality he brought to everything — thorough, fair, without drama in either the praise or the correction. Each student went through their form. The parents watched from the bench with the collective held breath of people who had invested in something and were watching the return.

When Maya's turn came she was third in her group. She took her position. Maya ran the form and she did not lose it. There was a moment in the second sequence where the hesitation was visible, where the body almost forgot what it knew. But it didn't forget. It found the next movement and committed and the form completed cleanly.

Mac said: "Good."

One word. Mac's version of everything.

Jennifer and Richie were both called in to help with the board breaking section of the test. Richie had a line of the older students that were testing and Jennifer assisted the younger children. She felt something she had not been prepared for as she watched her students, the Littles, smash through the wood obstacles she was holding for them. Jennifer

felt something she had not been prepared for; the clean sharp satisfaction and pride of watching someone succeed at something she had shared with them, lesson after lesson.

Sensei Mac wrapped up the test by informing the assembled students who had tested that they would receive their belts at their next class. He also mentioned that Sensei Marsh, Sensei Broomie, would be taking the final test in his pedagogy at 1:00. Nobody left the area.The words had barely left his mouth before it became clear that the question was not who would stay but whether there was enough floor space for everyone who intended to be there.

All of the chairs were taken and a dozen additional parents lined the walls. The junior students claimed the front. The Littles had arranged themselves in a cluster near office, Priya at the center with the specific authority of someone managing a situation. Kelly Williams was beside her. Nate Williams was against the wall with the older students, trying to look like he wasn't craning his neck.

Karen Williams was in the back with the other parents. She had her arms folded and her full attention on the mat.

Joe had come. He was near the door with his cane, which he didn't need on good days but had brought today. One of the high school students saw him arrive and made sure Joe had a chair. Jennifer was curious as to why Richie's desk chair was being rolled out of the room but it was clear when she looked, courtesy. "Chon-Ji," he said.
