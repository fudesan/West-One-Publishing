Prologue

What She Brought From Seattle

The thing about growing up in Seattle was that it calibrated you for a certain kind of gray. Not depressing gray — purposeful gray, the gray of a sky that was doing something, moving somewhere, carrying weather from one place to the next. Seacouver had the same quality. That was probably why it had felt like home inside of two weeks.

She packed the car in three hours — two hours and fifty minutes of actual packing and ten minutes of standing in the parking lot of her Capitol Hill apartment looking up at the window, deciding whether she felt anything about leaving it. She didn't, particularly. That was useful information.

The things she brought: a duffel bag of clothes running heavily toward flannel shirts, worn jeans, and band t-shirts in various states of age. A backpack with her laptop, textbooks, and a notebook she'd been filling since she was fifteen. A box of vinyl records she'd built one thrift store at a time since her cousin gave her a turntable for her seventeenth birthday. A drum practice pad and sticks. A coffee maker — a good one, purchased used from a musician moving to New York. Her skateboard, old and scuffed, Capitol Hill stickers from shops that might not exist anymore.

What she was going toward: a city she'd been to twice, a bartending job she'd gotten over the phone, an apartment found on a listing site and paid first and last month's sight unseen because the price was right and sometimes you just decided.

She got to Seacouver at three in the afternoon. She put the coffee maker on the kitchen counter first, then the turntable, then the records. She made the bed and hung her flannel shirts and sat on the floor with a cup of coffee and looked up the bar she was starting at on Monday.

Joe's Bar. Good reviews. Blues nights on Thursdays. The owner in a local arts piece, leaning on a cane in front of the stage, smiling the smile of someone who had built the right thing and knew it.

She showed up Monday at four o'clock.

The rest of it came after.

Year One

Chapter One — The First Thirty Days

May 6, 1991

Joe Dawson hired her in four minutes.

Jennifer knew it was four minutes because she had looked at the clock when she walked in — the reflex of someone who timed things, who kept track, who had learned that the intervals between moments were often as informative as the moments themselves. She looked at 3:50 pm when she pushed through the door. On time is early.

Joe asked two questions: How long did you work in Portland? Can you handle a Thursday night crowd alone?

She'd answered both truthfully — two years and yes, she could handle a crowd. Obviously it was easier with back-up but she had put down a few uprisings in her time. Jennifer asked if he wanted details. Joe declined the details. For someone who had just entered the lower-level checkered floor gin joint, Joe had a sense of her right away; good stock and most likely a good hire. Over time Jennifer learned that Joe looked at everyone the same way. He had the specific patience of a man who had spent decades watching people and who had learned to trust what the watching told him.

"All right, I'll see you tomorrow," Joe announced but his new hire did not move. "Something else? I don't think I have anything here for you that isn't a soft drink."

Old enough to serve, not old enough to enjoy. "Coffee?"

"Coming right up," Joe answered before stepping away. He caught her staring at the stage. "You play?"

She nodded and thanked him. "Yeah, that's really what drew me to your place. There's a lot of blues history here. It's almost a shrine."

Joe chuckled. "We worship daily."

And then she forced herself to ask. "I was hoping you'd consider letting me stay to get acquainted. My classes don't start for another ten days and I had to put down first and last months rent."

"Mondays are usually pretty slow around here, usually me and Leon," Joe returned gesturing to the kitchen.

She continued her plea. "My student aide won't come through until the beginning of next month," Jennifer added. "I promise I won't get in the way and I'll work for tips."

It didn't feel like a hustle. Before him was a young woman who just needed to get a few bucks in her pocket. "Sure, let me see you interact with some of the customers."

Monday ended up being somewhat successful, especially when Joe let her take the wheel. Jennifer left the bar with over forty bucks in her pocket and felt like a millionaire. She spent it all at the grocery store: quality coffee beans, whole milk, sturdy crackers, creamy peanut butter and one luxury box of Camels. With the change she had left Jennifer paused by the gum ball machines and weighed her options: candy, temporary tattoos or one of those super bouncy balls that defied the laws of physics. After turning the crank she received a plastic container holding a cerulean blue ball. She examined it and thought it might be an example of what the earth would look like if it was just a big sea of blue.

When Jennifer returned to her apartment she found a stowaway lime amid her groceries. She decided to name him Stowie and promptly elevated his rank to admiral of the refrigerator, Admiral Stowie. She put the blue ball next to Stowie so neither would be alone.

The first week was just work and between a call-out and the first impressions Jennifer had created, Joe let her come in every day. Jennifer learned the layout — speed rack configured left, well glasses on the right, the spot under the register where Joe kept the good Scotch for specific occasions. She asked what qualified as a special occasion and he said he'd let her know. Being there several days in a row Jennifer was meeting the regulars, getting to know their names, their preferred libations and made relevant small talk. She saw the rhythms of a bar that had been running long enough to have rhythms.

It was the fourth day, Thursday May 9th, when the tall man came into the picture. He arrived at five, was light on his feet on the stairs and planted himself at the right end of the bar. It was clear by his body language that the newcomer was the owner of that spot. Joe brought him a Scotch without asking. They said each other's names — Mac, Joe — with the compression of people who'd been doing this a long time. The man called Mac did a sweep of the room that she'd later learn was automatic and ingrained, a habit of someone who'd been reading rooms for a very long time.

He looked at her. She looked back. She was wiping down the bar and she didn't stop or make anything of it.

"New," he said to Joe. Not a question.

"Just started on Monday," Joe said. "So far, so good."

"Just a few days?" In Mac's opinion the new bartender looked like she had been there since the grand opening. She blended, fit in well. He looked at her again and caught her attention. "I'm Duncan MacLeod. And I think you've got Joe wrapped around your finger already."

"Competence recognizes competence," Jennifer returned before introducing herself. "Jennifer Marsh, fresh in from Seattle."

"Welcome Jennifer Marsh fresh in from Seattle."

Jennifer finished the section she was wiping and moved to the next. "You want anything else or is Scotch the plan?"

Mac smiled. Jennifer took that as an indicator to keep his cup full. Real, warm and a nice smile.

"Scotch," he said, "is always the starting point."

Jennifer moved down the bar, served two other people and did an effortless check back. Mac noticed the effort. "Thank you, Jennie," he said, without looking up from his phone.

Jennifer kept moving and wiped down the next section. She did not look back at Mac for a full minute and when she did he was still reading something on his phone. The man had Scotch at his elbow and was entirely unaware that he'd said anything particular.

Jennifer was aware. She had felt it land — the Scottish accent and the diminutive of her name, easy and unthought, like it had been in his mouth before he opened it. It was simply what he called her. It was a kind of deja vu she had never experienced until that night. No one calls me Jennie.

The rest of the shift went smoothly and nothing of major concern occurred. Joe said he wanted her to count the till and Mac realized it was time to settle up. "How much do I owe you?"

Jennifer looked to Joe for answer. "I'm sorry, I don't know how you handle tabs for your friends."

"Some of my friends pay nothing, some I give a discount and some I double charge," Joe returned. She had heard variations of that joke in the past. "Mac's in that first group. He drinks for free." Jennifer made a mental note and raised the bar of friendship. Soldiers in arms, perhaps, maybe family.

Mac had his wallet open and just put a fifty on the bar top. Grant. Fifty dollars. She could get a week of groceries if she planned it correctly. "Oh, that's entirely too much. I can't accept it."

"Perfectly good money," Mac said with a smile. She gave him a firm and final refusal. Mac put the bill back in his wallet and offered a solution. "How about I take you out to lunch tomorrow? Or give you the nickel tour of the city?"

Is this a date? Is this man asking me out on a date? "Not necessary, especially if you're a regular around here."

Joe shook his head. "Well, if you're not going to show up for a free lunch I will," Joe commented. "What time were you thinking?"

"Noon works for me," Mac stated before looking back to Jennifer. "Let me take you to lunch. It's clear you need a little financial help, otherwise you wouldn't be throwing your last days of summer away tending bar."

She felt an urge, a push to accept. Joe's comment sealed the deal. "Mac's a good man. Go to lunch. It's not like he's going to abscond with my new employee."

Abscond. Words made a huge difference when peppered into conversation like that. Those were destiny changing words.

Jennifer gathered up her things, asked Joe if she could take a to-go cup to finish off the coffee. He waved her on and watched as she left.

"I don't know what just happened here but I can tell you, this doesn't happen often."

Mac nodded. "She's special," he commented before sending out a group text.

I just met someone new at Joe's. Try to make it down here this week if you can. She's, she's something else.

* * *

Jennifer was waiting outside of Joe's with a board under her right arm, her bag and a cigarette burning. She had convinced herself that if the lunch date didn't work out that it would still be a good day. She enjoyed the ride to Joe's as the free-time fun she had been. The weather was perfect and she had work for the rest of the week. No going hungry.

"Nice deck." She turned and saw Mac coming around the corner. "Car's around back."

He waited for her and then made certain that she had the space closer to the building as her path. It was Jennifer's turn to comment. "Nice car," she commented. There was only one car in the back lot and it was a beautiful Thunderbird. "Is this a 57?"

"It is," Mac confirmed. He opened the car door for her, closed it and sat behind the wheel. "Ready?"

"Where?"

Once they were underway Mac took the opportunity at a stop light to turn on the radio and adjusted the dial. "What kind of music do you like?"

"All kinds, you name it. Blues, jazz, rock, grunge, what people call Classical and of course movie soundtracks." Mac noted that she had very eclectic tastes and let the dial fall chaotically. CCR was instantly adding to the carefree mood. Jennifer put an addendum on her answer. "All kinds, but not smooth jazz."

"All right, no smooth jazz," he echoed with a grin. Mac wasn't sure if that was a joke or a fact, but it was funny either way.

The T-Bird rolled into the Sea Cow parking lot and Mac selected a spot near the back of the restaurant and set his arm briefly on the passenger seat so he could turn his head and back into the spot. Jennifer instinctively moved out of the way. Get away parking.

Once parked Jennifer was trying to figure the logistics of opening the door with all of her gear on her right shoulder. Mac stepped around the car, opened the door and even offered a hand to help her step out of his chariot. I could get used to this. I could really get used to this.

Mac continued to lead the procession, held the door open for an exiting couple and then ushered Jennifer through the vestibule. He stepped up to the wait stand and requested a table for two. The manager showed them to a table, Jennifer sat first and put all of her gear on the long bench seat. Mac naturally across from her, scanned the room and then picked up the menu. He already knew what he wanted but it felt like the right thing to do.

Once at the table Jennifer unloaded her gear onto the long plastic seating. "What's good here aside from the fries?"

"Unfortunately it's all good," Mac said as he slid in across from her. "You really can't go wrong."

She nodded. "What's the budget?" A bus boy set two glasses of water on the table and a waitress followed. She asked if they wanted coffee. Jennifer said yes, Mac reversed his cup and said he would be fine with water. Once it was just the two of them again Jennifer repeated the question. "What's the budget today?"

"Order whatever you want, no budget," Mac answered. "I'm happy you came out. Let's enjoy ourselves."

She looked at him blankly. "All right, I'm going to order a Coke too with my meal."

Mac wasn't sure if she had the driest sense of humor he had ever encountered or was so strapped for cash that a second beverage was questionable. After they ordered Mac started investigating. "So, you said you're from Seattle and that you have classes coming up. What are you studying?"

Jennifer was about to answer when a woman in a mustard yellow uniform approached the table. When asked Jennifer requested coffee, black, and a Coke with a lot of ice. Mac indicated that he would be fine with water and checked to see if his guest was ready to put in a food order. Jennifer asked about the possibility of a veggie burger, settled on a grilled chicken sandwich instead with fries. Mac went with the standard lettuce, tomato and mustard burger. He indicated he also wanted fries. The waitress nodded, took the menus and departed.

"So, you were going to tell me about your new course of study." He listened as Jennifer went into the detailed plan that she had been building since she had made the decision to aim for a career in Library Sciences. Jennifer had purposely started at a community college to get the basics out of the way; just as informative and at half the price or more. Jennifer ended her five year plan by stating she was entertaining a Master's Degree, maybe even a PhD, but everything hinged on her successfully receiving the first degree.

Mac nodded. "One step at a time, that's how it's done."

Jennifer agreed and shifted the focus to Mac. "What about yourself? What do you do with your free time other than take out strange women to lunch."

"You're not that strange, not Portland-strange anyway," Mac commented which generated a smile. "I deal in antiquities mostly and co-manage a dojo."

Her brain could barely keep up with the knowledge dump. "A dojo, like a martial arts dojo?"

Mac confirmed that was exactly what he meant. "Are you familiar with martial arts?"

"No, not really. I took a few free self-defense classes at the Y to give me an edge at my previous job." Mac asked if that previous job had also been bartending and Jennifer said it had been. She had started as a bar back naturally, shadowed a real talent, rose up the ranks and was the weekend lead before she put in her resignation. "If you can picture a heavy metal bar attended mostly by privileged college students from UW along with some of the friendlier motorcycle clubs you'd know what my weekends looked like for the last two years."

Mac was trying to look through his catalog of drinking establishments but his train of thought was interrupted when two gigantic, oval white plates were served. Both Jennifer and Mac agreed that everything looked right and the waitress told them to flag her down if they needed anything.

"Sounds rough," Mac commented as he set both hands on his burger. He noted that Jennifer went straight to the fries.

"Rough place but some quality bouncers and a team that had my back," Jennifer responded continuing to ignore the sandwich. "Joe's is definitely a step up for me, much cleaner and so far, classier clientele."

"We aim to please." Why isn't she eating the sandwich.

"Which disciplines are taught at your dojo?"

"Everything," Mac answered before listing some of the options. "What interests you?"

"As it turns out, everything as well," Jennifer responded with a smile. She raised her hand to get the waitress's attention to get a box for the sandwich.

She's not going to eat the sandwich here because she's going to save it for dinner or for after a shift at Joe's. These purse strings are tight.

"I had a couple of thoughts on where we might go, depending on your needs."

Jennifer closed up the cover on her next meal and insisted that he had been more than generous to take her out to lunch. Mac countered that he had been her age once where every penny counted, had been fortunate enough to earn a living and believed in sharing the wealth. Jennifer got flustered trying to juggle her pride, her need and her curiosity over this new person, this new unusual person, that had just popped into her life. "I have a really hard time asking for help," she finally blurted in response. Her face flashed and she quickly regained composure. "I'm sorry, that was, that just slipped out."

"But, it's true," Mac parried. "You don't have to ask. I'm here. I want to help. What do you need for your apartment?"

Jennifer wanted to answer that she needed an apartment for her apartment, but agreed a few things wouldn't hurt.

From her general list of needs Mac decided the big box outlet was the correct choice. Jennifer hesitantly placed basics in the cart before attempting to remove it once she saw the price tags. "Stop that or I'll just start knocking things into this cart and go get another one."

Jennifer took direction easily.

Once they had chatted and loaded up on paper products Mac steered them into general home merchandise. "Give me an idea of the layout." Jennifer described her apartment as a box that had a bed and a few personal items strewn about. "That is strangely how I would describe my loft as well," he chuckled. "How are you on kitchen supplies?"

"A couple of coffee cups, a couple bowls, my coffee set-up and a record player, oh and my grinder of course." Mac cracked that he couldn't wait to hear about her furniture situation. "Oh, that's covered. The place came with a bed."

That wasn't a joke. She clearly had just rode her board directly from Seattle with what she could carry. "Well, they have these combo sets they sell now, four plates, four bowls, four coffee cups."

"I can only drink out of one cup at a time." True and wise beyond her years. He noted it and assumed that she didn't have silverware that wasn't plastic and made her choose between two different designs. When Mac started asking questions about pots and pans a little more sand fell out of the glass. "I, I really don't know how to cook. I don't need those."

"What if I want to come over and cook eggs."

Challenge accepted. "Dinner or breakfast?" she returned.

Mac picked a box of tools and set it in the cart. He was going to help her get all the tools she would need. It was a solemn, silent promise.

The only aisle that she didn't debate was the one that offered coffee. Once there, Jennifer put on a master class. She pointed out the three best options she saw for whole bean coffee, the ground brands that would do in a pinch and had a lot to say about Nescafe. Jennifer had basically been thinking aloud about how she planned to go about choosing a bag from the selection. Jennifer managed to stop a few other customers in their tracks with her announced preferences regarding coffee growing parts of the world. When one woman actually asked the lecturer a question, Jennifer pulled out her coffee notebook and happily shared what she knew. She encouraged the woman to take a picture of the coffee log. A natural leader, a teacher, vibrant when she's in her element. Who is this woman and why has she landed here? This is not just chance.

Once proper beans had been selected for everything they continued down the warehouse aisle into the coffee brewers proper. "Ah, there it is, the bane of my existence," Mac commented as they eased down the aisle. Jennifer followed his eye line and froze. "You have a Jura? You own a Jura and it's sitting in your house right now?" Mac confirmed he did. "I might just have to let you come over and make eggs."

"The machine does not like me," Mac announced. "It's a hostile relationship."

She just shook her head. "Well, if you ever consider getting a divorce let me know." Mac chuckled audibly. She restated it in her head and then attempted to apologize, as if that had crossed some kind of line. There was a real ebb and flow, clear and full of wisdom followed by periods of reluctance and fear.

"I suppose this would be a good time to ask if you are married."

"No, not married, no kids." She said she was in the same boat before asking for the time. No watch. "Well, it's after three and it's going to be a mad house checking out. What time does Joe want you at the bar tonight?"

After a few calculations Mac grabbed a few more pantry items, a Stanley thermos, travel cup and dish soap. "We'll look at the fresh produce and personal products next time."

Next time? We're going to do this again? Who is this guy?

Mac had sent a direct text to Richie requesting his assistance, if possible, at Joe's. Scheduled classes had finished for the dinner hours. Richie dropped everything, locked up and was waiting when Mac and his passenger arrived with a car full. He had received the text about the new woman at Joe's when it had been sent. He didn't realize that he was about to meet her immediately.

"What happened, Mac, did you hit a fire sale? One day only, everything must go?" Richie commented before introducing himself. "I'm Richie Ryan."

"Jennifer Marsh," she returned. As she freed herself from the car he made mention of her board. "You shred?"

Richie confirmed that he did indeed shred and suggested that they hit the skate park together at some point. She nodded and agreed. It was a date. "Is this all for the bar?" Richie questioned as he started grabbing items from the trunk.

"It's all going in the bar, back room for now, but these are Jennie's items," Mac answered simply. She silently thanked him for not adding that he had gone out of his way to pay for it all. It was hard enough to accept the help never mind find the help advertised.

Richie didn't make anything of it. Mac was a helpful guy, especially when it came to women. As she stood next to him unpacking the trunk Richie continued the small talk. "So, you're the new bartender Joe was talking about."

"I am indeed," she returned. "What's your go to beverage?"

"Depends on the time of day," Richie answered as he and Jennifer started walking to the bar. Mac had propped open the door which made the unloading and loading flow easily. "I need to get back to work shortly, a few more classes to go, but when it's quitting time there's nothing quite like a chilled long neck, especially in the summer."

"Classes as in dojo classes?" Jennifer questioned. "You're Mac's co-manager?"

"Mac's my co-manager," Richie insisted as he turned on the charm. "But yes, manager of DeSalvo's and we have two more classes before we close tonight."

"And what are you doing afterward?"

"Depends on if you'll have a chilled long neck for me." She assured him he would, thanked him for the assistance and sent him back to work with a fresh to-go coffee from the bar: cream, two sugars.

That man has the most incredible eyes I have ever seen. I could drown in those eyes.

* * *

Once all of the purchases had been handled Jennifer went through the routine she had constructed over the week. She checked everything, twice, to be certain they had everything clean and ready to go. She had a full bin of ice, all bottled items stocked fully, especially one bottle she marked as reserved. Joe wasn't anywhere to be found but Leon was in the kitchen ready to help out as needed. "And me as well, if needed," Mac offered.

Jennifer turned to thank him, checked his glass and then stumbled a bit on what to call him. "Thank you—"

"Mac," he insisted. "My friends call me Mac."

"Mac it is," Jennifer replied. It was a Thursday, jam night and she remembered Joe asking her if she could take on a Thursday night crowd on her own. Perhaps it was going to be that night.

A strange character of a man entered while she was checking on stage business. Everything was plugged in correctly and ready to roll. The band would take it from there. Jennifer went back to the bar and on the way noticed that the newcomer was sitting near Mac, at least, a friend. He gave off the same vibes she had been feeling around Mac all day as well as the dojo co-manager, Richie. This new character ordered a beer without looking at the menu or offering a greeting. Jennifer assessed him quickly; eyes that had seen too much and a face that had decided not to show it.

He put his feet on the bar.

Jennifer removed them. Without looking up, without comment, without breaking the rhythm of what she was doing. Mac was audibly chuckling. She was already moving to the next customer when she felt attention shift — not toward her exactly, but toward the fact that the thing the newcomer had done had produced a specific and immediate result.

Apparently Jennifer had met some criterion she hadn't known she was being tested against. It was disturbing as much as it was amusing.

"You're new," Methos said.

"Started on Monday," Jennifer replied. If Mac's friend wasn't going to introduce himself she figured it wasn't necessary to do the same as the place was really beginning to fill up. Music was advertised as starting at seven and Jennifer knew that meant half past the hour, at best. She kept the drinks rolling like she was an assembly line and everyone seemed relatively comfortable. It was a relief to see Joe enter through the back. "Ah, good. I thought this might be a test."

"Not a test but if it was I'd say you've done beautifully," Joe commented as he scanned the entire bar. There were no empty glasses, not a one, and she obviously had been bussing and serving simultaneously. "Take a break. It's about to ramp up in here."

Jennifer took ten, enough time to hit the restroom and the alley for a few puffs. By the time she was back behind the rail it felt like she had never left.

"So, this is the amazing new woman," Methos commented. "A little on the younger side for you but I can see the appeal. She certainly has everything that counts where it counts."

"We had lunch yesterday. Jennie's a nice girl."

Methos shrugged. "With a crowd like this your Jennie should make a killing."

"Why is there a long neck in the cooler with a reserved tag?" Joe questioned when Jennifer flew past him with a tub of glasses.

"Because it's reserved," Jennifer answered quickly and flatly before she filled everyone up before the music began.

Once the house band started the crowd chatter was outmatched. Jennifer picked up a bounce in her step to the twelve bar blues. She wanted to slow down, to watch a few people play, but work was work.

And Richie, dojo co-manager, showed up just as the second set was starting. "Oh, bar maid, I'd like an ale please."

"You got it, Richie," Jennifer returned before pulling the reserved beer out of the cooler, uncapped it and offered a glass. Richie declined the glass but was touched by the little sign she had taped onto a bottle, just for him.

"Hmm, maybe she's actually not your girl, after all," Methos announced as he watched the short interaction between Richie and the new girl. They were falling over themselves on a sticker, on a beer bottle.

"She's not anyone's girl, Methos," Mac countered. "But you can't deny there isn't something different about her. I can't put my finger on it."

"Everything will be revealed in time, Mac, everything in time."

* * *

Methos returned the following Tuesday. He put his feet on the bar. Jennifer removed them. Same result — no comment, no pause, no acknowledgment beyond the removal itself.

On the third Tuesday Jennifer was ready for it — not performing readiness, just awareness — and she had his feet off the bar before he'd fully settled them. Jennifer heard him chortle and wasn't sure if that was laughing at or with. The whole bar had heard him chortle. Jennifer filed the dossier on Methos focusing first on the way he read everything — phone, book, occasionally a newspaper folded with origami precision. The fact that he had put his feet on the bar three weeks in a row and she had removed them every single time was burned into her brain. Neither of them had said a word about it.

By the fourth week it was no longer a test. It was the shorthand, their established new shorthand that read: I see you, you see me, we have an understanding, now let's get on with it.

He called her Marsh on the fourth week, which was a different kind of shift in register — from new bartender to something more like: person I have assessed and found acceptable. She did not say anything about this. Jennifer simply received it and wondered who he had pestered to learn her name.

* * *

Fitz came in the third week like weather. Jennifer was immediately amused but didn't know why. Fitz put both hands on the bar, looked at her as though he had just won the lottery and asked the ultimate question. "When are we running away together, darling girl?"

"As soon as my shift is over," Jennifer returned. Fitz reached for her hand, placed a gentle kiss and then executed a charming gesticulation. "What might your name be?"

"It might be Jennifer Marsh but I supposed that depends on who is asking."

"I'm Hugh Fitzcairn. Everyone calls me Fitz. Give me the good whisky and I'll tell you how I won my name."

Jennifer quickly checked the notes Joe had given her detailing the guests she should just serve and not charge. She gave him the good whisky as requested and Fitz shared his story. When Jennifer was able to take out her journals and write she labeled him as charming.

The only name left on her crib notes was Amanda. One more and the whole gang would be there.

* * *

Amanda arrived and while Jennifer felt the something, it was much more mellow, noticeable but mellow. "You must be Amanda."

"You must be Jennifer," Amanda returned as she settled on a seat at the bar. "I've heard a lot about you and am so glad I get to meet you today."

Jennifer was a little flustered and couldn't help but wonder what the lady was trying to sell her.

"I wanted to know if you were worthy of the room."

"And?" Jennifer said. "Don't keep me waiting."

Amanda looked at her — at the canvas jacket, at the braid, at the quality Jennifer had that Amanda recognized because she had once had a version of it herself.

"I watched you hold the bar alone on two separate occasions and I've seen you handle nine difficult situations with finesse. And the face Mac has when contemplating you without you knowing he was there is quite something."

When an answer did not appear Jennifer asked Amanda for her drink order. Jennifer opened a Pinot Gris and poured a proper glass.

"You're worthy of the room," Amanda said after thanking her for the wine. "More than worthy. The room is yours. I just wanted to know before I decided how much I liked you."

"And?" Jennifer felt like only half of the conversation was in English.

"I like you very much," Amanda said. "Which I find slightly annoying, for reasons that have nothing to do with you."

They talked for an hour. Amanda told her three things about Mac — nothing private, nothing that crossed a line, just the texture of someone who had known for a while. And while it was nice to get some background on Mac, the reason Amanda felt compelled to share it with her was unclear. When she was able to jot some notes in her journal about Amanda, Jennifer just wrote the word: WHAT?!

Chapter Two — The Wrist-Lock and the Dojo

The incident happened on Wednesday, June 12th. Jennifer was working alone on the slow end of an evening shift when the man three stools down from Mac decided that her arm was something he was entitled to grab. It wasn't.

The moment the patron's hand touched her skin she removed it. From there she executed a basic, outward wrist rotation. In two seconds she had removed his hand and had his wrist at an angle that communicated clearly without causing injury, and then she stepped back and said, "Don't."

He didn't. She went back to work.

Jennifer was aware, without looking, that Mac had seen the entire encounter. His quality of attention was different from others, in and out of the small circle of Joe's friends. Mac's interest was complete, patient and genuine. She'd felt the attention shift when the man grabbed her arm and she'd felt it stay but relax as she handled it.

Jennifer didn't look at Mac. She served the next person.

At close, when only Joe and Mac were left, Mac said, "Where did you learn that?"

"Seattle," she said knowing he probably wasn't looking for a geographical reply. She was counting the till and was not about to be distracted. "Self-defense class in high school."

"Just the one class?" Jennifer replied that the one class had been enough for its intended purposes. She stated that she knew what she knew, and was more than aware of the things she did not. Mac turned his Scotch glass. "Do you want to know more?"

She looked up and stared at him for a moment. "I always want to know more."

"It's always best to start with the basics and build out from there." This was it. This was the moment of clarity. Joe's new bartender had been dropped into Mac's lap so that he could train her. She didn't read as a pre-immortal to him so the why was still hidden.

"That works for me. You're the one with this thing of commitments. Do you even have an empty space to stuff training in there as well?"

The dojo was on Morrison Street, a converted industrial building three blocks from the bar. DeSalvo's Martial Arts was still faintly visible on the exterior. Jennifer arrived on Tuesday, June 18th just before eight in the morning. The street door was unlocked. Jennifer let herself in and from the hallway she could see that Mac was already there, in training clothes, doing something with a wooden sword — a bokken, she would learn — with an economy of movement that demonstrated years of practice.

Jennifer remained in the hallway. She didn't want to interrupt and figured Mac would greet her when he was ready.

"Shoes off before you cross the threshold," he said. "Always. The floor is sacred space."

Jennifer set down her bag, removed her shoes and saw there was a pair near the door lined up neatly to the wall. She placed hers next to them.

Jennifer turned back to the threshold. She bowed — a small, genuine act of respect, that communicated she was taking it seriously. "Good," he said. "The same when you leave."

Jennifer entered and went to the center of the training space. There were clean lines built into the structure, some weapons parked on the wall and high windows. The space was large and well-maintained. "The cleaning," Jennifer said. "What needs doing?"

"We'll get into that later. Come stand here facing me and bow in." Mac explained the process of bowing in and how the practice changed depending on the type of class or lesson was using the space. For their purposes a short bow from her to her teacher would suffice countered by a return bow reaffirming the commitment to the purpose. "I will teach you everything I know, for as long as you want to learn."

I karate teach, you karate do.

"I agree to those terms, opposite and inverse," Jennifer announced. "I will learn everything you can teach me for as long as you want to train me."

Mac cupped both hands over his sternum and instructed her to do the same. "This is how we'll start and end our lessons. Calm yourself, slow your breathing and clear your mind. Close your eyes and focus on breathing. When you can feel, better yet, when you can hear your heartbeat, this is the mantra I am giving you today: I am still. I can hear my heartbeat. When you're centered and ready, you'll stand and we'll begin."

Jennifer calmly found her center and repeated the mantra in her mind. Three times felt correct. She stood and looked at Mac for the next step.

"Let's do some warmups and then you can show me what you can do."

Mac was aware that she could execute the wrist lock and offered himself as the target. Jennifer performed the action three times in succession, the same way as she had in the bar and Mac assessed every detail before offering critique. "Your instincts are good but your form needs work. Your balance is better than your form, which means the form is fixable. Did the self-defense instructor demonstrate the counter move an opponent would likely make?"

"There's a counter move?"

"There's always a counter move," Mac replied. "You might've gotten lucky the other night. Had that clown known how to get out of your hold things could've gotten dangerous."

Jennifer shrugged. "You were there. I wasn't worried and figured you'd do the whole damsel in distress routine if things got out of control."

"But I won't always be there, as much as I am subscribed to the damsel in distress routine," Mac stressed. "You need to know the offensive move as well as the defensive, both sides of the same coin."

"Okay, if I do the wrist lock again will you show me the counter move?"

"I will but it's going to involve me touching you, nowhere untoward, and you're going to land on the mat." Jennifer agreed to the terms and found herself getting really acquainted with the mat. It truly was a good thing she had never used the technique with someone that knew more than she did.

They worked for a full hour. Once Mac was satisfied that she was getting the idea of how the wrist lock could be exploited he showed her Horse Stance and ran down the basic blocks and kicks, one by one and then together in a simple kata. He was glad to see she knew how to form a punch, showed her how to start with her hands at the waist and had her perform her punching technique high, middle, low. She had good rhythm.

Mac ended the class as they had begun. Jennifer put her hands on her sternum and controlled her breathing. I am still. I can hear my—

The rattle of the lift drew Jennifer's attention and she lost the moment. "Well, look who it is bright and early," Richie announced. "You're the new cleaning crew?"

"We're in the middle of something, Richie," Mac sighed. Richie checked his watch, nodded and slipped away into his office. "Let's do that again."

Jennifer closed her eyes again, took a breath and placed her hands, centered herself. I am still. I can hear my heartbeat.

Jennifer knocked on the office door. It was open, but manners were manners. "Hey Richie, Mac said you had some paperwork for me."

Richie began shuffling mail, file folders and an assortment of papers until he found the ones he needed. "Just basic information we ask students or their parents to fill out," he announced before handing her the new student packet. Jennifer took a pen out of her bag and started filling out the paperwork. "How did the lesson go?"

"Good, really good. I know more now than I did before I came in," she answered. "Mind if I sit down?"

"Not at all, make yourself at home." Home. Jennifer questioned if Richie lived in the same building referencing the lift. "My apologies on that. Mac told me he had a private student this morning and would be finished at nine."

"What time is it now?" Jennifer was surprised to hear it was nearing ten. "I guess time really does fly when you're having fun."

Richie nodded and smiled along with her. "Oh, to your question, I don't live here, Mac does. His lift is on the top floor. My place is near the court house."

Jennifer looked up and tried to picture where that was on the city map that had been slowly filling in since her arrival. She couldn't picture it until Richie provided some landmarks. "So, am I going to be talking to you regarding cleaning or Mac?"

"Probably both of us but for now I'd say the most important thing would be sweeping the floor and wiping down any equipment, if you have time today."

"I do have time today but my summer is winding down soon," Jennifer sighed as she paused on the forms. "Gotta get it all in now before things get really crazy."

Richie grinned and leaned toward her. "We should go to the skate park then. When are you available?"

Jennifer swept the floor with the large broom that lived in the corner in the back of the training area. The feel of the wood was noticeably different after sweeping it. When she was finished Jennifer put the broom back in its place, remembered to bow out of the dojo and slipped on her shoes.

"Same time next week," Mac said.

"Same time next week," she confirmed.

On Monday June 24th Jennifer was already at the skate park. Richie hadn't expected that. Board under her arm, jacket on despite the warmth, Marlboro going, looking at the bowl with the expression of someone calculating something. He stopped at the top of the ramp. "You're early."

"You're late."

Richie looked at his watch. He was three minutes early. He dropped in without responding. This was, he'd already learned, sometimes the correct move with Jennifer.

Jennifer was better than he'd expected. Not better than him but better than the bar suggested. At the bar Jennifer operated as someone careful, considered and economical. The skate park suggested someone who had made her peace with falling a long time ago and had built her whole game around that philosophy. She ate it on the third run. A clean wipeout, nothing dramatic, the board going one way and her going the other. Jennifer rolled and came up looking at where the board had gone.

Richie was beside her before he'd thought about it. Jennifer insisted that she was fine before he could say anything. "I know, you tucked the roll." And after a brief pause. "Where'd you learn that?"

"Seattle. Concrete there is unforgiving."

"Tell me about it," Richie commented as he offered a hand up and handed her the board.

They took a break on the concrete edge with water bottles in hand.

"You're good," Jennifer announced. It wasn't a compliment exactly, more like a notation.

"So are you," Richie returned.

"I'm decent. You're good."

Richie looked at his board and turned it in his hands. He had the expression Jennifer had started to catalog as: the one that meant he was deciding whether to say something. "I've been doing it a long time."

"How long?"

"A really long, long time."

She understood that they were stumbling over an edge and didn't push. "I started at thirteen. Capitol Hill. Self-taught."

"It shows," Richie commented and then caught her look. "That's a compliment. Self-taught means you read the surface. Coached skaters sometimes miss that."

She considered this. "It's the drum thing."

Richie was confused until she started air drumming. "You play drums. Of course you do."

"Reading in advance," she said. "Committing before you have the whole picture. It's basically the same thing."

He looked at her. The quick eyes, doing the thing they did when something landed. "Yeah," he said slowly. "Yeah, that's exactly what it is."

Walking out of the park, Jennifer fell into step beside Richie without discussing it. His board under his arm. Hers under hers.

At the entrance she stopped. "I need to get moving. My shift starts within the hour."

"I can drop you," Richie offered.

She looked at the motorcycle and at him with the specific assessment of someone running the logistics. "The boards," she argued.

Richie thought about it for approximately two seconds. He went to the bike, opened the saddlebag, pulled out a bungee cord, looked at the two boards, looked at the bungee cord, looked back at Jennifer. "This is going to work."

"Is it?"

"Probably."

It worked. Barely. The boards were bungeed to the back with the specific precariousness of something held together by optimism and exactly one bungee cord. Jennifer looked at the arrangement for a moment. "If those fall off—"

"They won't."

"If they—"

"They won't," Richie insisted before issuing an order. "Get on."

She got on.

Jennifer had ridden on motorcycles before, but not often and not like this. The city moved past quickly and her hands found somewhere to be. The specific quality of being behind someone on a bike which was different from every other kind of close in the world. Jennifer put her hands at his waist. Not tight. Just there.

He didn't say anything. Neither did she.

Joe's came up in seven minutes. Richie pulled to the curb and Jennifer got off the bike. He unhooked the boards — both intact, bungee cord still holding — and handed hers over. She took it. She looked at him.

"Same time next week. Put it in your schedule," he said. Casual. Like it was already decided.

She looked at him sideways. "Bold and efficient." Richie almost smiled she almost smiled back. "Same time next week."

Jennifer went inside. Richie sat at the curb for a moment longer than he needed to before pulling away.

He thought about the tucked roll for the rest of the afternoon. He thought about how correct her hands felt at his waist. Both things. In no particular order.

Inside, Methos was at his stool. He had been at his stool for the last twenty minutes. He had a direct line of sight to the front window. Jennifer came in, board under her arm and went straight behind the bar. Methos said nothing. She tied on her apron and checked the speed rack. She did not look at him. He waited.

"Not a damn word," Jennifer managed after a minute turned five.

"I didn't say anything."

"Your face was involved in a three way conversation."

"Interesting turn of phrase, Marsh." He picked up his beer. He looked at it with the specific attention of a man examining something he found very interesting. "I'm simply sitting here. As I often do."

She moved down the bar. He took a sip and slowly set the glass down like it was a gavel. Case closed.

Methos waited until Jennifer came back his way. "You know," he said, "the motorcycle is a courtship display."

She looked at him.

"Across most of recorded human history, the offer of transportation has been a reliable indicator of romantic intent. The horse. The carriage. The—"

"Methos."

"I'm providing context. It's what I do."

"I needed a ride to work, he had a motorcycle. End of story."

Methos sat for a moment. "Where were you with Richie that you needed a ride to get here?"

"We were at the skate park," Jennifer answered giving the cross streets of that area.

"So a date."

"Two people who like to skateboard skateboarding."

"Was this prearranged? Did you two discuss these plans beforehand or was it a happy accident?" Jennifer told him the discussion had started at the dojo and they had looked at their calendars to nail down a date. "So a date." Jennifer said nothing. She wanted it to count as a date but wasn't sure if Richie had the same accounting system. "Richie has two legs and could have walked you here. He chose the motorcycle and offered it to you. He also bungeed two skateboards to the back of it, which suggests either optimism or a desire to impress. I suspect both."

She looked at him with the flat voice fully deployed. "You watched the whole thing," she sighed.

"I was sitting at the window. You drove directly past it."

Jennifer groaned. "You watched the whole thing."

"The bungee cord was a bold choice. I'll give him that."

She moved away and he watched her go with the expression of a man who had been watching people fall in love for five thousand years and found it, without exception, completely predictable and completely interesting every single time.

Well at least his damn feet weren't on the bar this time.

"How many people have a key to this place?" It wasn't a real question. Methos finished his beer and ordered another. Nothing more was said about the matter of the motorcycle. Jennifer moved through the early shift thinking the issue had been put to bed.

Mac entered with greetings, sat down and a Scotch was served without asking. Methos waited thirty seconds. "She arrived on Richie's motorcycle." Mac looked up at Jennifer briefly and then put his gaze on Methos. "They bungeed the skateboards to the back. Both of them. One cord."

Now would really be a good time for that damsel in distress thing.

"Methos, it was a ride."

"It was a date."

"Maybe it was, maybe it wasn't. Leave it alone."

Methos didn't know how to leave anything alone. "Marsh and Ryan are dating."

"We'll let you know when to hire the band and rent the hall."

"I'm simply reporting," Methos said. "You know how I feel about accurate documentation."

Mac picked up his Scotch and evaluated Jennifer with the specific expression of a man quietly revising his understanding of a situation he had already been watching closely.

The Quickening

Joe's Bar — Week Three

It started as nothing she could name.

A frequency. Wrong, or right in a way she'd never had access to before. There and gone before she could turn it over and look at it properly. Jennifer looked at the door. She looked at the room. Everything where it should be. She filed it. Something for later.

And everything went out. Not gradually. All at once — the lights, the amplifiers, the refrigeration hum behind her, the specific electrical silence of a room that had been running and was now not. The band stopped mid-bar. Someone at a table was startled.

Jennifer was already moving.

Candles, second cabinet, below the bar. She knew where they were because she'd put them there herself and done so because she'd worked bars long enough to know that power went out and you kept candles where your hands could find them in the dark. She found them in the dark.

Jennifer lit candles. She moved through the room with the matches and the votives and set them on every surface and the room went from dark to amber and the customers who had half-risen settled back down because the bartender was clearly still working and there was something about a woman moving through a room with calm and candles that clearly stated that the matter was under control.

Some left anyway. She thanked them, meant it and moved on.

The band huddled. She caught the guitarist's eye. "Acoustic?"

He looked at the room, the candlelight, and the customers resettling. The atmosphere was actually perfect for an acoustic set.

Joe appeared from the back with his own guitar. She was already behind the bar, lining up the last votives, already thinking about the frequency, already deciding it could wait. The bar was open. The candles were lit. She was professional and she had handled blackouts before and whatever the frequency was it would still be there after close. She would write it out then.

The door opened.

Methos came in a little too fast for a man who had heard about a downed power line.

He didn't go to the bar. He didn't sit down. He stood just inside the door with his hands in his pockets and looked at the room — the candles, the customers, Joe on his stool with the guitar, Jennifer moving behind the bar — with the specific quality of a man waiting for a bus he already knew the schedule of.

She clocked him immediately. She kept moving. Glass, candle, rag. Professional.

He stood there.

Jennifer went to his end of the bar. "Can I get you anything?"

"No."

She looked at him. He looked back. The ancient eyes doing the thing they did — present, reading, filing. She filed him back.

Jennifer moved away. She came back two minutes later. Methos was still standing in the same spot with his hands in his pockets looking like he had nowhere to be and was in no hurry to get there, which was the least convincing performance she'd seen since Fonzie jumped the shark.

The back door opened and Mac came through. Methos looked at him. One look — the full inventory, quick, top to bottom. Mac met it. Something passed between them that had no words and took approximately one second. Methos nodded and left. Just like that. Door open, door closed, gone, as if he had never been there for any particular reason.

Jennifer stood behind the bar in the candlelight and watched the door settle shut and then she looked at Mac.

Mac looked at her with the expression of a man who had things to say and was deciding how to say them.

"Two glasses when we close." It was an invitation to let it all settle and discuss later. Mac agreed, sat down and she set his Scotch in front of him without being asked.

The guitar played. The candles burned and lit up many faces. It had been a while since she had enjoyed an acoustic set.

Mac sat at the bar the way he always sat at the bar — coat over the back of the stool, both hands around the Scotch, unhurried. Like he'd come in from an ordinary evening. And Jennifer let him have it. She had customers, candles to tend and glasses to refill. The band had found its groove and the room had settled into the specific warm ease of people who had decided a blackout was an improvement on their original plans.

Jennifer was good at her job. She did her job. At some point she came back to Mac's end of the bar. She topped up his glass without being asked and he watched her do it. She didn't look at him. He looked at her anyway. The full version — not the Mac who ordered Scotch and read his phone, but the Mac who had four hundred years of reading people and was reading her now, quietly, without making anything of it.

She could feel it. She'd been able to feel it since the first week.

Jennifer set the bottle down and met his eyes. Neither of them said anything for a moment. The guitar played. A candle guttered and steadied. Somewhere in the room someone laughed at something.

"Are you all right?" he questioned lowly.

She thought about it honestly. "Yes. Are you?"

"Yes."

A beat.

Jennifer nodded. "Good. There's no way I'm going to be able to learn the falling thing without you." She picked up her rag, moved away slowly and then over her shoulder, "Or the one inch punch."

Mac sat with his Scotch. Something in his chest that had been very tight for the last forty minutes loosened considerably. "No," he said, quietly, to the bar. "You're probably not."

Mac stayed until closing. She gave him the last of the Scotch without being asked. He accepted it without comment.

When Joe had locked the door and gone to count the till she leaned on the bar across from him with coffee in hand and looked at him for a moment. "You ever see Cinema Paradiso?" Mac indicated he had watched it several times. "Alfredo, the projectionist, he knows the whole time. He knows exactly what Salvatore is and what he's going to be and what he needs." Jennifer took a sip of her coffee. "And he never says it directly. He just — tends to it. From the side. Without making it a thing."

Mac said nothing. "And Salvatore figures it out, eventually, in his own time." She focused on Mac. "That's a good way to do it."

"It's a good film," he said carefully meeting her eyes head on.

"It's a great film, one of the best," Jennifer said lifting her mug in salute to movie magic. "I'm not pushing. I just want you to know that I know there's something to push upon."

Mac looked at her for a long moment. "Noted."

"Good. Two glasses again soon."

Mac gave her a ride home. He watched her go up the steps with her bag and her board and not once looking back, and he sat at the curb for a moment after the door closed.

He thought about Alfredo, about the reel of film. The kisses cut out one by one over thirty years, saved up, given back at the end when there was nothing left to protect the boy from. The love that expressed itself entirely in what it arranged and never in what it said.

Mac had done that. He had done versions of that across four hundred years — the ones he'd loved well and sent forward, into their lives, into their futures, away from him because away from him was where the future was. He looked at the door she'd gone through. Jennifer was not going to be one of those.

He didn't know yet what she was going to be. He didn't know what the frequency meant, what Joe's file said or what Methos had been turning over behind his beer since she had come into their lives. Mac didn't know what the two glasses soon ritual was going to cost him when it finally happened.

But he knew that much; Jennifer was not going anywhere.

He drove home and did not think about Alfredo again.

July 8 — The Skate Park, The Stairs, The Apartment

The Descendents Evening

It was the third Tuesday.

She'd brought Gatorade this time — orange, the original, the correct one — because the second Tuesday had ended with her sitting on the edge of the bowl feeling the specific consequences of two hours of skating in summer heat without hydrating. It was a rookie mistake and she was not a rookie, she'd just forgotten.

Jennifer was drinking it at the top of the bowl when Richie dropped in beside her, board under his arm, slightly out of breath from the line he'd been working on the far side of the park.

He looked at the Gatorade. "Orange."

"Obviously."

Richie sat down. He looked out at the park — the usual Tuesday crowd, a few kids on the flat ground, a guy in his thirties who came every week and was quietly, steadily getting better at something he'd started too late and didn't care.

"In my experience," Richie said, with the tone of someone opening something carefully, "most skaters that drum have very strong opinions about the Descendents."

She looked at him. She looked back at the park.

"I think that's a very leading question," she said.

He smiled. She caught it in her peripheral vision — the quick bright one, the unguarded one. "It's not a question. It's an observation."

"It's an observation designed to provoke a specific response," she said. "Which makes it a leading question wearing an observation's clothes."

"Okay." He stretched his legs out. "Do you have opinions about the Descendents."

"That's a question."

"That's a straight question," he said. "No leading. Just asking."

She drank her Gatorade. She looked at the park.

"Milo Goes to College," she said.

He went very still in the way of someone who had asked a thing and gotten an answer that was going to require full attention.

"Yeah," he said slowly.

"1982," she said. "Fourteen songs, twenty-seven minutes. They understood something about economy that most bands spend their whole career trying to figure out and don't."

"Okay but I Don't Want to Grow Up—"

"Is a perfect song," she said. "I'm not arguing that."

"You say that like you're about to argue something else."

"I'm going to argue," she said, "that Milo is the document and everything after is the commentary. Which is not nothing — the commentary is good. But the document is the document."

"All is literally called All," Richie stated. "Like they named it that on purpose. Like they knew."

"They knew they were making a very good album," she said. "They didn't know they were making Milo. Nobody knows when they're making Milo. That's what makes it Milo."

"That's circular."

"It's accurate."

He pushed off the ground with his heel, rocking slightly. He was thinking. She could see him thinking, the quick eyes working through it.

"The drums on Milo," he said.

"Bill Stevenson," she said. "Yes."

"He was seventeen."

"I know how old he was."

"Seventeen," Richie said again, with the emphasis of someone for whom that number had a specific texture. "And he played like that."

She looked at him. There was something in the way he said it — not wistful exactly, something more specific than that. The particular quality she'd been cataloguing since week three, the not-quite-rightness of his relationship with time. Seventeen landing differently for Richie Ryan than it did for anyone else in this skate park.

She let it sit. She didn't push it.

"He played like that," she agreed.

They skated for another hour. The argument continued between runs — she took the position that the early catalog was the peak and he took the position that this was the kind of thing people said about bands they'd loved young and were protecting, which was not analysis it was nostalgia, and she said there was nothing wrong with nostalgia when the object of it had genuinely earned it, and he said that was the most dignified defense of nostalgia he'd ever heard, and she said thank you, and he laughed.

The sun was just beginning to set when they picked up their boards.

The Stairs

Jen lived eight blocks from the park, about a mile, and she hadn't mentioned it. Richie fell into step beside her in the natural way of someone who wasn't ready for the conversation to end and wasn't going to make anything of it. She didn't make anything of it either.

"The 1982 pressing," he said.

"Yeah," she said.

"Of the Bad Brains record."

"That's the one we were discussing."

Not can I come up. Not pushing anything. Just — sometime. If she wanted.

"We're not done with this."

Third Floor

Jennifer reached the door of her building and her left quad informed her, with no ambiguity, that it was done. Not cramping. Not sore in the ordinary way. Done, in the specific way of a muscle that had been working for two hours and had used up everything it had and was now filing a formal complaint. She kept her face neutral. She had a lot of practice keeping her face neutral, but her voice was anything but neutral. Jennifer let off a string of expletives that would have made a trucker blush.

Richie was back in no time, up the stairs and ready to help. "Which one is yours?"

By two in the morning the room had arranged itself the way rooms arranged themselves when nobody was managing them. Mac was in the corner armchair. Fitz was on the couch with the specific horizontal commitment of a man who had decided. Amanda had tucked herself into the booth with her coat as a blanket. Methos was still reading.

Richie had gone to the pull-out at midnight. Jennifer had gone upstairs at half-past. Joe did his last check of the bar — the bottles, the taps, the till — the routine of someone who had been closing this bar for years.

He turned off the main lights. Left the low ones on. Sat down in his chair behind the bar.

He looked at his bar on Christmas Eve — the bottles catching the low light, the tinsel, the small tree on the end of the bar that Jennifer had decorated on the Wednesday with the specific care of someone who had not expected to be asked and was therefore doing it properly.

Amanda, from the booth, with her eyes closed: "Good night, Joe."

"Good night," he said.

Mac, from the armchair: "Merry Christmas, Joe."

"Merry Christmas," Joe said.

Methos turned a page. He said nothing. He had the expression of a man who did not do sentiment and was expressing it through the quality of his continued presence, which was the Methos version of the same thing.

Joe looked at the tree.

Jennifer had put a small star at the top. Paper, folded carefully, the kind that took time. He hadn't seen her do it. She'd just — done it, at some point, and there it was.

He sat with his bar for a while. Then he went upstairs.

* * *

Jennifer came down first, because Jennifer always came down first. She found the bar quiet and the low lights still on and Mac asleep in the armchair with the specific dignity of someone who had been asleep in armchairs for four centuries and had perfected it.

She went to the kitchen. She made coffee. She made enough coffee. She came back out and put a mug on the side table next to Mac's armchair without waking him.

She sat at the bar.

She looked at the tree.

She drank her coffee.

Mac woke at seven with the immediate alertness of someone whose body had not forgotten four hundred years of needing to wake up ready. He looked at the bar. He looked at Jennifer. He looked at the coffee on the side table.

"Good morning," he said.

"Merry Christmas," she said.

He picked up the coffee. He looked at the tree.

"The star," he said.

"Paper," she said. "I found some in the back."

He looked at it for a moment.

"It's good," he said.

"Thank you," she said.

They sat with their coffee in the quiet of Christmas morning and the bar was still and the tree had its paper star and outside Seacouver was doing its gray purposeful thing and inside Joe's bar it was completely right.

* * *

One by one the others came down. Fitz arrived in the kitchen looking for tea and found Jennifer had already made it. Amanda emerged from the booth fully composed. Methos came down the stairs with his book and the expression of a man who had arrived exactly when he intended to.

Richie came down last with his hair doing its morning thing and his eyes doing the half-open thing and found coffee already waiting and Jennifer already at the bar and sat down next to her with the ease of someone who had been sitting next to her for months and intended to continue indefinitely.

He looked at the tree. He looked at the star.

"Did you make that," he said.

"Wednesday," she said.

"It's good," he said.

"That's what Mac said," she said.

"Mac has taste," Richie said.

She handed him his coffee.

* * *

Joe came down at eight and stood in the doorway of his bar and looked at the assembled squad occupying his Christmas morning — Mac in his armchair, Amanda in the booth, Methos reading, Fitz making something in the kitchen that smelled like it involved butter, Jennifer and Richie at the bar — and had the expression of a man who had not planned any of this and was finding it to be exactly right.

He went behind the bar. He made himself a coffee. He stood at his end.

"Merry Christmas," Jennifer said, without looking up from her mug.

"Merry Christmas," Joe said.

* * *

The white elephant happened first. Joe had a box of contributions. Nobody was entirely sure whose idea the white elephant had been — Jennifer's probably, because it had the fingerprints of someone who had researched the optimal structure for a group gift exchange and implemented it without announcement.

Methos drew first. He opened his gift with the unhurried quality of someone who has been unwrapping things for five thousand years and has opinions about the process. Inside was a kazoo.

The bar went quiet.

Methos looked at the kazoo. He looked at the room. He looked at the kazoo again.

"No," he said.

"White elephant rules," Richie said. "You keep it or you steal."

"I decline both options," Methos said.

"Those aren't the rules," Jennifer said, from behind the bar.

He looked at her.

"Those are absolutely the rules," she said. Flat voice. No negotiation.

Methos looked at the kazoo. He put it in his pocket. He had the expression of a man making a strategic retreat with maximum dignity.

He stole Amanda's gift four turns later. She had gotten a 1962 Bordeaux that Methos had identified in approximately three seconds as something he needed more than she did.

Amanda acquired the kazoo.

She looked at it. She looked at Methos. She put it in her bag.

It was never seen again. This was the correct outcome.

* * *

Mac and Fitz had the decorative spoon situation.

It was a decorative spoon. Enamel handle. Some kind of crest or scene that had become illegible over the years. It had been traveling between them since 1857 in increasingly elaborate wrapping. By 1991 the wrapping alone took four minutes to get through.

Mac opened it. He lifted out the spoon.

He looked at it.

He turned it over.

"Aye," he said. Quietly. With feeling. "I haven't seen one of these since I was a wee lad."

He ran his thumb over the enamel. He had the expression of a man holding something that connected him to something lost. The room was very still.

Jennifer, who had been watching this, felt something move through her chest. She looked at Fitz. Fitz was watching Mac with the warm patience of someone who had given a meaningful gift and was receiving the meaning back.

She looked at Joe. Joe was looking at his drink with tremendous concentration.

She looked at Richie. Richie was looking at the ceiling.

She looked at Methos. Methos met her eyes and immediately looked at his beer.

She looked back at Mac, who was still holding the spoon with four hundred years of Scottish feeling.

"Mac," she said. Gently.

"Aye," he said. Still looking at the spoon.

"That's a beautiful spoon," she said.

"It is," he said. "It truly is."

A beat.

"Where is it from," she said.

The longest pause.

"I have absolutely no idea," Mac said.

The room detonated.

* * *

Jennifer looked at Fitz through her tears from laughing.

"How long," she said.

"1857," Fitz said, with the dignity of a man who had been waiting one hundred and thirty four years to see that face on someone who didn't know.

"What is the spoon," she said.

"We don't know," Fitz said.

"Where did it come from originally," she said.

"We don't know that either," Mac said. He was holding the spoon. He was not putting the spoon down.

"Then how did it start," she said.

Mac and Fitz looked at each other.

"He gave it to me," Mac said.

"He gave it to me first," Fitz said.

"You gave it to me in 1857," Mac said.

"You gave it to me in 1856," Fitz said.

"That's not —"

"I have documentation," Fitz said.

"You do not have documentation," Mac said.

"I have a letter," Fitz said. "It mentions a small gift. The spoon was the small gift."

"The small gift was a book," Mac said. "The book came separately."

"Methos," Jennifer said.

"1857," Methos said, without looking up. "Mac gave it to Fitz. I was there."

A beat.

"You were there," Fitz said.

"I was adjacent," Methos said.

Mac pointed at Methos.

"Adjacent," Mac said. "He's always adjacent."

"The spoon," Jennifer said, because she needed to know, "is going back in the wrapping next year."

"Obviously," Mac said.

"The wrapping takes longer every year," Fitz said. "Last year alone —"

"The wrapping is part of it," Mac said.

"The wrapping is the best part," Fitz said.

Jennifer looked at the spoon in Mac's hand. She looked at the man holding it. She looked at the room.

She got out Orangi-San later and wrote four pages. The spoon got its own entry. It always will.

* * *

The Saturnalia punk happened mid-morning, after the toast and before the Super Nintendo.

Jennifer came into the living room with her coffee. Completely casual. The flat voice at the warmest setting.

"Happy Saturnalia, Mithros," she said.

The room went quiet.

Methos looked up from his book.

"It's December twenty-fifth," he said.

"Saturnalia ran through the seventeenth," she said. "I know. I'm a little late. I've been busy."

He looked at her.

"I did some reading," she said.

She proceeded to wish Mithros a very happy Saturnalia with several details slightly wrong. The dates slightly off. The specific rituals slightly confused with other festivals. The gift-giving tradition slightly muddled. She delivered it all with complete sincerity.

He corrected her. Gently at first. Then with more specificity as the errors kept coming. He couldn't help it. Five thousand years of knowing and she kept getting it just wrong enough that he had to say something.

He was three corrections in before he noticed.

She had called him Mithros every single time.

He went very still.

She looked at him with the flat voice and the full version of herself.

"Not quite as titillating as mummies and werewolves," she said. "Yet incredibly satisfying."

The room. Mac had the expression. Amanda was delighted. Joe was not smiling with maximum effort. Fitz had no idea what had just happened and was laughing anyway.

Richie, from the floor where he was opening a box: "Did she just punk Methos?"

"Yes," Mac said.

Methos looked at her for a long moment.

"Well played," he said. Just that. The Methos version of a standing ovation.

"That's my girl," Richie said. To the floor. Quietly. Not for the room.

Joe heard it. Joe filed it. Joe picked up his cloth.

* * *

The Super Nintendo came from Jennifer's bag. A large bag. She had been carrying it since she arrived.

She put it on the bar in front of Richie.

He looked at it. He looked at her.

"Happy birthday," she said. "It's close enough to January."

He looked at the box. He had the expression of a twenty-one-year-old man receiving a Super Nintendo on Christmas morning at Joe's bar.

"Jennifer," he said.

"It just came out," she said. "I heard it was good."

He looked at the box. He looked at her. He kissed her right there in front of everyone.

The squad reacted in character. Methos said nothing and filed it. Amanda said "finally." Joe picked up his cloth. Mac had the expression. Fitz raised his glass.

Fitz, who had missed several key developments by being in London, looked at the kiss, looked at the room, looked at Methos.

"Have I missed something," he said.

"Several things," Methos said. "Since approximately August."

"August," Fitz said. "And nobody told me."

"You were in London," Mac said.

"I have a telephone," Fitz said.

"Fitz," Jennifer said, from the bar.

Fitz looked at her.

She looked at him with the flat voice and the warmest setting.

"Merry Christmas," she said.

He looked at her. He looked at Richie.

"Merry Christmas," he said. With feeling.

He picked up his tea. He sat down. He was smiling for the rest of the morning.

* * *

The Soundgarden necklace came later, privately, when the room had arranged itself into smaller conversations and nobody was watching specifically.

Richie put the small box in her spot on the bar. No announcement. No ceremony.

She opened it.

A guitar pick — Soundgarden, the real thing — encased in sterling silver on a silky black thread. Not precious. Not delicate. Something functional made permanent. Something chosen for what it was and what it meant.

She looked at it. She looked at him.

She put it on right there. Didn't make anything of it. Just put it on and touched it once to settle it against her collarbone and went back to her coffee.

He watched her put it on. He had the full version of his expression.

She wore it for the rest of Christmas. She wore it the next day. She wore it after that.

She would not take it off for years.

* * *

Later — Christmas afternoon, the bar quiet, the three o'clock rule still an hour away — Jennifer and Richie were on the floor of Joe's living room with the Super Nintendo. Joe's television. Joe watching from his armchair with his coffee and the expression of a man who has two twenty-year-olds playing video games in his living room on Christmas afternoon and has no complaints.

She looked at the necklace. She looked at Richie.

"Are we going steady," she said.

He looked at her.

"I mean," she said. "This is essentially your class ring."

He looked at the necklace. He looked at her with the full version of himself.

"Do you want to be going steady," he said.

"I'm wearing your class ring," she said. "On Christmas Day. In front of everyone."

"You put it on yourself," he said.

"You gave it to me," she said.

"I did," he said. Not looking away.

She looked at him for a moment.

"So," she said.

"So," he said.

A beat.

"Yeah," he said. "We're going steady."

She nodded. She looked at the Super Nintendo. She picked up her controller.

"Good," she said.

He picked up his controller. He was smiling for the rest of the day.

Neither of them told the squad. The squad already knew. Joe filed it anyway.

* * *

At three o'clock Joe said nothing. Nobody moved. Nobody had anywhere to be that was better than here.

The rule had done what the rule was supposed to do.

The first Christmas at Joe's. Which meant there would be others. Which was the point.

Outside Seacouver did its December thing. Inside the bar the low lights were still on and the tree had its paper star and it was Christmas and everyone was where they were supposed to be.
