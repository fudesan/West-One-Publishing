ONE — ESKRIMA

After the Solstice — DeSalvo’s

Mac put two rattan sticks on the mat.

Jennifer looked at the sticks. She looked at Mac.

“FMA,” Mac said. “Filipino Martial Arts. Eskrima, Kali, Arnis — the terms are related. The principles are shared. The core of it is ambidextrous weapon use — both hands, simultaneously, independent.” He looked at her. “Pick them up.”

She picked them up.

She held them. She felt the weight distribution — rattan, light, roughly the length of her forearm, balanced differently from a bokken, differently again from a sword. She turned them. She felt how they moved.

Her hands moved with them the way her hands moved with drumsticks.

Mac was watching her.

“You’ve been doing this for years,” he said.

She looked at him.

“The independent hand coordination,” he said. “Ambidextrous control, different rhythms in each hand simultaneously. You’ve been training that since you were fifteen.” He paused. “I should have seen it sooner.”

She looked at the sticks. She looked at her hands.

Drumsticks, she thought. This is drumsticks with consequences.

“Why now,” she said.

Mac looked at the mat.

“Because what we found out at the solstice changes the training timeline,” he said. “If you’re pre-Immortal — if you are what we think you are — then at some point you’re going to be in a situation that requires a response. And we don’t know when. And we don’t know—” he paused. “We don’t know if you can die the way a mortal dies. We don’t know if you’ll come back. We don’t know the rules for someone like you because there’s never been someone like you.” He looked at her. “So we prepare as if the rules are the most dangerous possible version. And we do it now.”

She looked at the sticks.

“The katana,” she said.

“Still in the curriculum,” he said. “But this is better for you. Lighter. Faster. Two weapons means two attack angles simultaneously. Harder to read. Easier to conceal.” He looked at her hands. “And you already know how to use them. You just don’t know you know yet.”

She turned the sticks. She found a grip. She moved through something that was not quite a kata — something that her hands knew from years of a different practice, translated into this context, arriving intact.

Mac watched.

He had the expression of a teacher watching a student arrive at something that had been there all along.

“Yes,” he said. “Exactly that.”

She went again.

The sticks moved the way she needed them to move.

She filed it in the place where she kept the things that felt like home.

Jennifer asked it between sets. Not dramatically. The way she asked things that had been sitting in the filing system and had finally risen to the top.

“Is the dojo holy ground?” Mac stopped. “Is it holy ground?”

“No.”

“Can it be made holy ground?”

Mac was quiet for a moment. He looked at the mat. He looked at the dojo. “It’s not—” he started. “The designation isn’t something you create. It’s inherent to the history of a place. A church is holy ground because of what it’s been used for and what it means to the people who use it. A mosque. A burial site. The ground holds the meaning.” He paused. “DeSalvo’s has been — it’s a place of practice. Of genuine practice. Over time—”

“Over time it might be,” she said.

“I don’t know. I’ve never encountered a dojo that became holy ground. It’s not something I can make happen. I’m not sure anything I can do makes it happen.”

She looked at the mat.

“Methos would know,” she said.

“Methos would have a theory,” Mac said. “Which is different.”

She smiled. The small one.

“Why are you asking,” he said.

“Because I’m going to be spending a lot of time here,” she said. “And I want to understand what I’m in.”

Mac looked at her. “What you’re in,” repeated.

“This world,” she said. “Your world. My world now. I want to know the edges of it. Where the protections are and where they aren’t.” She looked at him. “If I can’t make the dojo holy ground what do I do instead?”

“You fill it with people who would protect it,” he said. “People who know what it is and what happens here and who would not allow the Game to come inside it. You’ve been doing that since you started sweeping the mat.”

She nodded and picked up her sticks. “Again?”

“Again.”

THREE — MRS. WILLIAMS

“In America,” Jennifer said carefully, “we say Zee.”

“In Scotland,” Mac said, equally carefully, “we say Zed. In Britain. In most of the English-speaking world that is not America, in fact.” He looked at Richie. “Zed.”

Richie turned to Jennifer. “He’s been saying our alphabet wrong this whole time.”

“It’s not wrong,” Mac said. “It’s correct.”

Jennifer was looking at her notebook. She had written it out. She said it quietly under her breath, Zed-anshin. She said it again. “Zanshin Dojo.”

The office was quiet.

“That’s the name,” Richie decided.

Jennifer turned the notebook so Mac could see it. Zanshin Dojo. And underneath, in smaller letters: the awareness that doesn’t stop.

Mac looked at the notebook and nodded. “Yes,” he said. The quiet version. The one that meant more than yes.

She turned the notebook back around. “I’ll take the building, on one condition,” Jennifer announced. “DeSalvo’s name goes somewhere in the building, permanent. The history of the building matters and I want to honor what it was before it was mine.”

“Zed,” Richie sighed, trying out the word. It had good mouth feel as it was short and definite.

“Zed,” Jennifer said.

Mac looked at the two of them. “Zed.”

The paperwork took six weeks.

This was faster than Jennifer had expected, slower than Richie had expected and exactly as long as Mac had expected. Mac had been involved in various forms across various centuries and had learned that legal processes moved slowly regardless of what you needed from them.

The evening it was finalized Mac called both Jennifer and Richie upstairs. Champagne and dinner were at the ready.

Jennifer came up first, “You cooked.” She immediately began setting the table.

“It seemed appropriate.”

Richie was two minutes behind Jennifer and stopped in the doorway. He was pleasantly surprised by what he saw. Once the table was ready Mac asked them both to sit down. Mac poured the champagne, three glasses and raised his. “To Zanshin Dojo.”

“To Zed,” Jennifer agreed.

Richie raised his glass. “To Zed.” He looked at Mac. “And to Mac saying Zed and being right about it.”

Mac looked at him. “I’m always right about it.”

They sealed the toast.

The dinner was excellent and the champagne was correct. Outside the loft windows November Seacouver was going about its business, and inside the three of them ate and chatted.

After dinner Jennifer was looking at the folder on Mac’s desk. Not going through it. Just looking at it. Her name on the cover page, Jennifer Anne Marsh. The legal language that turned a building into a possession and a possession into a responsibility.

“Can I ask something? Is this still your loft?”

“The loft is part of the building,” he said carefully.

“So technically?”

“Technically.”

“I’m your landlord,” Jennifer announced.

The loft was quiet.

Richie looked at Mac. “Are you a squatter?”

“I am not a squatter,” Mac countered. “I am a long-term resident with an established claim to the premises.”

“That’s what a squatter says,” Richie replied before he turned to Jennifer. “Does he need a lease? Is there a deposit situation?”

“There’s no deposit situation,” Jennifer said. She was looking at the folder. She turned to Mac. “But I feel like there should be paperwork.” Mac looked at her. “For my own protection, as the property owner.”

“Your protection,” Mac said.

“Legal exposure, I should probably understand what I’m liable for.” Jennifer turned the folder over in her hands. “Richie, does he have renter’s insurance?”

“I don’t need renter’s insurance,” Mac interjected.

“Everyone needs renter’s insurance,” Jennifer said.

Mac looked at the two of them and drank more of the champagne. “I will,” he said, with the complete dignity of a man making a significant concession, “look into renter’s insurance.”

“And I’ll need first and last,” Jennifer said.

“You absolutely will not,” Mac protested.

She held his eyes for three seconds. Then the thing happened at the corner of her mouth. “We’ll discuss it.”

“There’s nothing to discuss.”

“That’s what the squatter always says,” Richie said into his champagne.

“Does this put me in a different tax bracket?” Jennifer continued.

The loft held the three of them with the specific warmth of an evening that was going to be one of the many they all held closely.

Jennifer picked up her glass. “Same time Tuesday.”

“Same time Tuesday,” Mac echoed with a true grin.

“To Zed,” Richie said.

They drank.

THE PRIVATE BELT

Zanshin Dojo — Wednesday, August 19, 1994

The text came in at two-seventeen.

Impromptu session, mandatory, before Joe’s. Come as you are.

Jennifer looked at it twice. She had just gotten out of her afternoon seminar and had forty minutes before her bar shift. When Ki-san called, she went.

The dojo was empty.

Mac was at the supply closet. That was all — just Mac, the closet, the specific quality of an afternoon dojo with nobody in it. Richie wasn’t there. This registered. Richie was usually somewhere in the building at this hour, the specific background presence of him. His absence was noted.

Jennifer removed her shoes at the threshold, bowed and entered the space.

Mac said nothing. She crossed to the mat, found her position, and lowered herself into seiza — 正座 — the formal sitting, feet tucked, hands on her thighs, spine straight. She closed her eyes.

*I am still. I can hear my heartbeat.*

Jennifer heard Mac move. She tracked him the way she had learned to track movement in a room; without looking, without the eyes, the awareness that had been opening in her since before she had a name for it. Mac crossed the mat and stopped in front of her.

Jennifer opened her eyes, stood and bowed.

He returned the bow. Then he gestured — a small, specific motion, her arms, parallel to the floor.

She moved her arms and Mac stepped in.

Jennifer understood what was happening at the moment his hands found the belt and she nearly froze. The nearly was all there was. The freeze did not complete itself. Jennifer had learned too well the thing Mac had taught her about commitment: you go all the way through or you don’t go.

Mac worked with the economy of someone for whom this was not a ceremony but a fact being made visible. He tied the belt correctly — the knot, the tuck, the specific tension of something that was meant to hold. When it was right he gripped both ends and gave a final tug, precise, the period at the end of a sentence.

Mac stepped back and looked at her. Then he placed his right fist against his left palm.

Jennifer understood and brought her hands together. They bowed and the session closed the way all sessions ended.

No words had been spoken. It was one of the sessions that said the most.

Jennifer bowed at the threshold on the way out, put her shoes on and walked to Joe’s. She was four minutes early for her shift with a black belt neatly folded in her bag.

BELT TESTING DAY

Zanshin Dojo — A Saturday

Jennifer arrived early as usual to make coffee and train with Mac. Richie arrived with breakfast at 7:30 as he usually did on a Saturday morning. Jennifer started another pot of coffee and wolfed down her meal. It was going to be a big day, one that had been advertised for weeks and she wanted to make sure everything went off without a hitch.

The floor needed to be right, the wood clean and responsive, the corners clear. Jennifer had mopped it the evening before and moved as much of the free weights and other equipment flush to the windows. That opened the space beautifully. Everything was in order by 8:15. The building already had the specific buzzing quality of an event that mattered. Parents arrived at eight forty-five. The younger siblings trailed behind with the specific philosophy of younger siblings everywhere: be present, don’t get in the way, wait for something interesting.

The students arrived in their uniforms, cleaner than usual, pressed. She recognized this quality because she had checked her own uniform the night before with the specific ritual of preparation that indicated the intention was serious.

Maya arrived at 8:52, found Jennifer by the supply corner and said nothing. Jennifer looked at her and nodded once. It was an acknowledgment that said: you know the form. Your body knows the form. Let it. Maya nodded back and went to join the other students lining up.

The testing itself was precise and sequential. Mac ran it with the quality he brought to everything — thorough, fair, without drama in either the praise or the correction. Each student went through their form. The parents watched from the benches with the collective held breath of people who had invested in something and were watching the return.

When Maya’s turn came she was third in her group. She took her position. Maya ran the form and did not lose it. There was a moment in the second sequence where the hesitation was visible, where the body almost forgot what it knew, but it didn’t forget. It found the next movement, committed and the form completed cleanly.

Mac responded, “Good.” One word. Mac’s version of everything.

Jennifer and Richie were both called in to help with the board breaking section of the test. Richie had a line of the older students that were testing and Jennifer assisted the younger children. She felt something she had not been prepared for as she watched her students, the Littles, smash through the wood obstacles she was holding for them. She had a clean sharp satisfaction and pride in watching her students succeed at something she had shared with them, lesson after lesson.

Sensei Mac wrapped up the test by informing the assembled students who had tested that they would receive their belts at their next class. He also mentioned that Sensei Marsh, Sensei Broomie, would be taking the final test in his pedagogy at 1:00. Nobody left the area. The words had barely left his mouth before it became clear that the question was not who would stay but whether there was enough floor space for everyone who intended to be there.

All of the chairs were taken and a dozen additional parents lined the walls. The junior students claimed the front. The Littles had arranged themselves in a cluster near office, Priya at the center with the specific authority of someone managing a situation. Kelly Williams was beside her. Nate Williams was against the wall with the older students, trying to look like he wasn’t craning his neck.

Karen Williams was in the back with the other parents. She had her arms folded and her full attention on the mat.

Joe had come and was near the door with his cane. One of the high school students saw him arrive and made sure Joe had a chair. Jennifer was curious as to why Richie’s desk chair was being rolled out of the room but it was clear when she looked, courtesy. She ate just enough lunch to squash hunger pangs and filled up on water. Richie kept her company and also ate some lunch. “You’re going to do great, Jen. None of them have left. If anything more people have arrived.”

“Not helping with the nerves, Richie.”

He nodded, placed his hand over hers and set it gently on her sternum, “We are still. We can hear the heartbeat.”

Promptly at 1:00 Mac stood in front of the crowd and provided a short biography of his student, Jennifer Marsh.

While Mac shared his speech she ran a check over herself. Jennifer had her hair up, tight, the working version and her uniform looked sharp. She was ready and gave Mac a small nod. He continued, “Before we begin, there are three parts to this exam. Jennifer is going to perform a complete set of forms. Each one will be called by name. I’m asking you to hold your applause until I give the signal at the end.” He looked at the room. “All of it, and then all at once.”

The room absorbed this. Nobody argued.

Mac looked at Jennifer.

“Chon-Ji,” he said.

She moved.

Mac called them clean and even, one after the other, and she met each one. Dan-Gun. Do-San. Won-Hyo. Yul-Gok. Joong-Gun. Toi-Gye. Hwa-Rang. The room was completely silent.

And the silence accumulated. Each form landed in it and the lack of sound held the form grew heavier, more specific, more aware of what it was holding. The parents who had come expecting a children’s class recital were three forms in and understanding that this was something else entirely.

Choong-Moo.

Jennifer moved through it the way she moved through things she had learned, completely. Her hands were precise and her stances were low and rooted. When she struck Jennifer struck with the whole of herself, with the specific commitment of someone who had been told hundreds of times that half a technique is no technique. She finally, completely, understood.

The Littles were very still. Priya had her hands folded in her lap.

Mac watched. His face gave nothing but to someone who knew how to read it, everything.

Ge-Baek.

The last form. Jennifer worked through it, arrived at the finish and held the final position. The room held with her.

Mac raised his hand and the room came apart.

The sound was specific, not polite applause or the scattered clapping of people being courteous, but real enthusiasm. Jennifer stood at the end of the mat and breathed. She was fierce and entirely herself. Jennifer had sweat on her forehead, her braid had held together and her eyes were bright.

She bowed to the room. The room bowed back.

Mac cleared the floor while parents and students moved closer to the walls. Richie came onto the mat and Jennifer went to meet him. They bowed to each other and the sparring section of Jennifer’s test began.

“Hands only,” Mac said. “Light contact. Three rounds.” The room went quiet again.

Richie moved first, fast and economical, the years with Mac in his body. Jennifer read him the way she’d learned to read him, which was differently from the way a stranger would have seen him. Jennifer knew his tells and Richie knew hers. The sparring between them had always been a conversation conducted in a language they’d developed together.

First round. Jennifer gave ground when she needed to and took it when she could. They were, the room could see, genuinely evenly matched in the specific way of two people who had trained the same curriculum under the same teacher.

Second round. Mac set the terms for the second round, “Legs only. And keep those hands up, Jennie.” Richie, who had been counting on the arm drop, recalibrated.

Third round. “Hands and legs.” Richie moved in fast and was relentless. This was the last round and he wanted to see Jennifer bring her best to the mat. This was her show and she needed to shine. When Richie amped up his offense Jennifer matched him strike for strike. Richie did land a couple of kicks she hadn’t blocked efficiently, but it was Richie who hit the mat. For one moment his focus went somewhere it shouldn’t have and Jennifer’s combo landed. There was a group gasp and then the room went completely silent again.

Jennifer was already crouching beside him, hand out. Richie found the not-quite-smile, the warmth underneath it that was specifically his and his alone. Richie took her hand, she pulled him up and then bowed to each other. The match was complete.

The room made a sound that was not quite applause and not quite a collective exhale but somewhere between the two.

Mac said nothing. He had seen what he needed to see.

The boards were pine. Mac held two of them together, horizontal, at waist height. The room had pressed back to give space. The Littles were watching with their whole bodies. Jennifer stood at the correct distance, found her alignment and took a full breath.

Mac said, “When you’re ready.”

Front snap kick.

The middle of both boards left Mac’s hands at speed. He stood holding the top edges and the bottom edges of two boards that no longer had middles. There was a beat where the room processed what had just happened. Then the appreciation.

Jennifer reset. Richie moved to her left with a board. The older student, a sixteen-year-old named Daniel who had drawn the assignment by virtue of being the largest junior student, took position to her right. Mac stood at the center.

Three boards. Three positions. A crescent arc.

“When you’re ready.”

Jennifer turned and moved with speed. Three kicks formed an arc so fast that the boards fell essentially simultaneously. There were three snaps in the specific time it took to blink, and then three people stood holding the top portions of boards that no longer had bottoms. Daniel looked at his hands, the piece of board he was holding and then at Jennifer, at Broomie. His expression was priceless.

Jennifer went for her water bottle. She took thirty seconds at the side of the mat, breathing and shaking out her hands. Mac stood beside her and spoke quietly. “Follow through,” he said. “All the way to the floor. Don’t think about the concrete. Think about the floor underneath it.”

“I know,” she said.

“You know,” he confirmed. “Do it.”

Jennifer handed him the water bottle and walked back to the mat. Richie and Daniel had set the concrete block. The room had gone very quiet. The Littles had stopped moving entirely. Priya held Kelly’s hand.

Jennifer stood before the block. She did two slow practice movements — the alignment, the arc of the strike, all the way through, no power, just the geometry of it. Once. Twice.

She set her feet and made the sound. It was the specific noise of a person committing the entire body to a single purpose. Jennifer’s hand came down and the concrete hit the mat. The room produced a sound that was not organized into words.

Jennifer straightened, checked her hand and verified that she had broken the brick. She took a breath.

From the direction of the Littles came the specific sound of eleven children for whom professionalism had reached its absolute limit. They were across the mat before Mac could clear it, the entire cluster of them, and Jennifer went down under the weight of them, not as a fall but as an arrival; the kind that knocks you backward with the specific force of children who have decided that this moment requires them to be physically attached to you.

Mac stood at the edge of the mat and scanned the room. The entire dojo had become delightfully undone. He let it run for a moment. “Floor.”

One word. That was all it took. The Littles peeled off with the speed of children who knew that voice. Jennifer got to her feet. She was laughing — the real one, from the right place. The room was still going.

Mac picked up the black belt from the table at the side and crossed over to Jennifer. The room hushed on its own.

Mac tied the belt himself, the way he had tied it the first time with no one watching. He tied it correctly, with the specific economy of someone for whom this was not a ceremony but a confirmation of something already true.

Mac stepped back and beamed at her. “Jennie,” he said. “Well done.”

Jennifer held his eyes for a moment. She nodded once — the small nod, the one that meant what it meant between the two of them. She then turned to the room.

Karen Williams was clapping with her whole arms now. Nate was beside her. Kelly had gotten loose from Priya somehow and was at the front of the room with her hands above her head.

Richie was at the very back. He was not clapping but watching her proudly. He had known this was coming and was still undone by the arrival of it. Jennifer found him across the room, fell into his arms and he planted one on her. There were a couple of reactions from a couple of the kids, but the kiss was correct. It was true and truth lived in the dojo.
